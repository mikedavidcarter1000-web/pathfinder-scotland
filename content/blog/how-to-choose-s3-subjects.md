Picking your S3 subjects is the first big decision of your senior phase. It feels enormous because adults keep telling you it's important. The truth is more reassuring: S3 choices matter, but they don't lock you in forever. This guide walks you through how the system works, what to think about, and the mistakes to avoid.

## What actually happens at the end of S2

Until S3, you've been in what Scotland's [Curriculum for Excellence](https://education.gov.scot/curriculum-for-excellence/) calls the Broad General Education (BGE). You study a wide range of subjects to give you a foundation. At the end of S2, your school asks you to narrow that range so you can start working towards qualifications.

Most schools ask you to pick around 6 subjects on top of the compulsory ones. Almost every Scottish school keeps English and Maths compulsory through S3 and S4. Some schools also keep a modern language or PE compulsory. The exact pattern varies by school, but the shape is the same everywhere.

## The eight curricular areas

Your school is meant to give you breadth across the eight curricular areas defined by the [Scottish Government](https://education.gov.scot/curriculum-for-excellence/curriculum-areas/):

1. **Languages** — English, modern languages (French, Spanish, German, Mandarin), Gaelic
2. **Mathematics** — Maths, Applications of Maths
3. **Sciences** — Biology, Chemistry, Physics
4. **Social studies** — History, Geography, Modern Studies, Politics, Sociology
5. **Expressive arts** — Art and Design, Drama, Music, Dance
6. **Technologies** — Computing Science, Design and Manufacture, Engineering Science, Graphic Communication, Practical Woodwork, Practical Metalwork
7. **Religious and moral education** — RMPS (Religious, Moral and Philosophical Studies)
8. **Health and wellbeing** — PE, Home Economics, Health and Food Technology

The idea is simple: you should leave S3 with at least one subject from most of these areas. Not because every area is equally interesting to you, but because dropping a whole area at 14 closes doors you don't yet know exist.

## How the column system works

This is the bit that confuses everyone. Schools don't let you pick any 6 subjects you like. Instead, they put subjects into columns — usually 5 or 6 columns — and you pick one subject from each column.

| Column 1 | Column 2 | Column 3 | Column 4 | Column 5 |
|----------|----------|----------|----------|----------|
| History | Biology | Art | French | Computing |
| Geography | Chemistry | Music | Spanish | Business |
| Modern Studies | Physics | Drama | German | PE |

The columns exist for timetabling reasons. They mean you can't always have your perfect combination. If you want both Biology and Chemistry but they're in the same column, you can take one but not the other. This is normal and it isn't your school being awkward — it's a real constraint on how the school day is built.

This is why you're asked to rank your choices and pick reserves. Don't sulk if you don't get column priority for everything. Schools try hard, but the maths sometimes makes it impossible.

## Why breadth matters more than you think

Here is the most important thing in this entire guide. You don't need to know what you want to do. Most 14-year-olds don't, and that's fine. What you do need to do is keep options open.

A student who keeps a science, a humanities subject, a language, and a creative or technical subject through S3 has every door still open at the end of S4. A student who drops every science to focus on what feels easier has, without knowing it, closed off medicine, dentistry, vet, engineering, computer science, biology, biomedical sciences, geology, sports science, forensic science, and dozens of other paths. None of those decisions feel like decisions when you're 14 — they feel like "I'm just dropping Physics."

## How to think about pathways without knowing your career

Use this rule of thumb. For each subject you're considering dropping, ask: **what am I closing off?**

If you drop a modern language, you make some university degrees harder to get into and you may regret it if you ever want to live, study, or work abroad. If you drop a science, you close STEM degrees. If you drop English at any level above National 5, you make almost every degree harder. Maths is the same.

Try the [Pathfinder pathway planner](/pathways) to see what each subject combination opens and closes. You can sketch a possible S3 timetable and see which Highers it leaves available, which courses become possible, and which careers it points towards. Doing this for half an hour will tell you more than any leaflet your school sent home.

## Common mistakes

**Dropping a language too early.** Languages are seen by universities as a sign of intellectual breadth. Russell Group universities used to require a language at GCSE for some courses, and Scottish universities still treat them as facilitating subjects. If you take French, Spanish, German, or Gaelic to National 5, you keep your options open. Drop it in S3 and you'll find it almost impossible to pick up later.

**Avoiding sciences "because they're hard".** Sciences are not harder than other subjects — they're harder if you don't enjoy them. The same is true of every subject. If you're capable of working at it, taking at least one science to National 5 keeps a huge number of degrees on the table.

**Picking subjects to be with friends.** You will not be sat next to your friend in every lesson, even if you're in the same class. You'll see them at break and lunch regardless. Don't choose Geography over Computing because Maya is doing Geography.

**Listening only to one teacher's advice.** Your teachers are experts in their own subject. The Maths teacher will tell you Maths is essential. The Art teacher will tell you Art is essential. They're both right and both wrong at the same time. You need a balanced view, not one teacher's enthusiasm.

**Picking only "useful" subjects.** Universities and employers value enjoyment as much as utility, because students who enjoy their subjects do better in them. A subject you love and get an A in beats a "useful" subject you slog through for a C every time.

## What if you genuinely don't know

If you really cannot decide, follow this rule: pick one subject from each curricular area, and pick the broadest possible combination. That gives you maximum options at the end of S4. You can then specialise later, when you know yourself better.

A safe broad S3 might look like:

- English (compulsory)
- Maths (compulsory)
- One science — pick the one you find most interesting, not the one you think is "best"
- One humanities — History, Geography, or Modern Studies
- One language — French, Spanish, German, or Mandarin
- One creative or technical subject — Art, Music, Drama, Computing, or Design

This isn't a magic combination. It's a default. Tweak it for what you like. The important thing is the shape — wide rather than narrow.
