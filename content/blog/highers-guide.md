The Higher year is the year that matters most for university applications. Most Scottish universities make their offers based on Higher results from S5, not S6. This guide walks you through how many Highers to take, which ones carry the most weight, and what grade levels different universities expect.

## How many Highers do you actually need?

The honest answer: most students take 5 Highers in S5. Some schools allow 4. A small number of strong students take 6, though this is unusual and the marginal benefit of a 6th Higher is small.

For university entry, what matters is the grade pattern. Almost every Scottish university course expects 5 Highers, ideally taken in S5. Some courses will accept 4 if the rest of the application is strong, but you're working against the grain.

Why S5 specifically? Because Scottish universities make conditional offers in early spring of S6. They want to see your S5 results to confirm you're on track. If you're hoping to "save" Highers for S6, the offer is very hard to get in the first place.

## Which Highers carry the most weight?

The Russell Group used to publish a list of "facilitating subjects" — subjects they considered most useful for keeping university options open. The list has been retired in name, but the underlying logic still applies. The subjects that open the most doors are:

- **English** — required or strongly preferred for almost every degree
- **Maths** — required for STEM, economics, business, computing, finance, and many social science degrees
- **A modern language** — French, Spanish, German, Mandarin, or Gaelic
- **Sciences** — Biology, Chemistry, Physics
- **History or Geography** — recognised humanities for social science, law, and arts degrees

This doesn't mean other subjects are worthless. Computing Science, Business Management, Drama, Art, PE, Music, and many others lead directly to specific degree pathways and careers. The point is that the subjects above are the most flexible — they keep the most doors open if you're not yet sure what you want to do.

[Informed Choices](https://www.informedchoices.ac.uk/) is the main UK resource for understanding which subjects unlock which degrees.

## Grade requirements by university tier

Scottish universities are not all the same when it comes to entry requirements. Roughly speaking, they cluster into tiers — though every course is different and there are exceptions to every rule.

### The most competitive

The University of Edinburgh, the University of St Andrews, and the University of Glasgow tend to ask for the highest grades, particularly for popular courses. Typical S5 offers for in-demand degrees:

- **Medicine, Dentistry, Vet** — AAAA (sometimes AAAAA)
- **Law, Architecture, Computing Science** — AAAB to AABB
- **Most arts and humanities** — AABB to ABBB

### The next tier

The University of Aberdeen, the University of Strathclyde, the University of Dundee, and Heriot-Watt University tend to sit one notch lower in published offer levels. Typical:

- **Highly competitive courses** — AAAB to AABB
- **Most degrees** — ABBB to BBBB

### Newer universities

Edinburgh Napier, Robert Gordon University, Glasgow Caledonian, the University of the West of Scotland, and Queen Margaret University often accept BBBC to BBBB for many courses, with some asking for higher grades for specific competitive programmes (Nursing at GCU, for example, fills quickly).

### Important caveats

These ranges are broad. The actual entry requirement for any specific course is published by the university and you should always check it directly — Pathfinder pulls the published requirements for every course in our database, so you can [check yours here](/courses).

## Does it matter if you take Highers in S5 or S6?

Yes — for competitive courses, it matters a lot.

When you apply through UCAS, your application is reviewed in autumn or winter of S6. Universities can see your S5 results (which are real and final) but only your S6 predictions (which are aspirational). For an oversubscribed course, an admissions tutor will trust the real grades much more than the predicted ones.

A student with five A grades from S5 has a much stronger application than a student with two A grades from S5 and three predicted A grades for S6. Even if both end up with the same final result, the first student gets the offer and the second doesn't.

For less competitive courses this matters less. For Medicine, Dentistry, Vet, and the most popular humanities degrees at Edinburgh and St Andrews, it matters enormously.

## Crash Highers — pros and cons

A "crash Higher" is taking a Higher in a subject without first taking the National 5. Most schools allow this in S5 or S6, particularly in subjects like Modern Studies or Politics where the National 5 isn't a strict prerequisite.

**Pros.** You can pick up a useful subject that you didn't take earlier, sometimes for a specific university requirement (Higher Maths is the classic example). Crash Highers in S6 are a great way to add a fifth Higher to a four-Higher S5 picture.

**Cons.** You're learning two years of content in one year. For subjects with a heavy practical or technical component (Chemistry, Physics, languages), this is very hard. For more conceptual subjects (Modern Studies, History, Politics, Geography) it's much more manageable.

The general rule: crash a humanities or social science subject if you need to. Don't crash a science unless you're already very strong in the National 5 content from a related subject.

## Advanced Highers — when you need them, when you don't

Advanced Highers are the qualification level above Highers. They're equivalent to the first year of a UK university degree in difficulty.

**You need Advanced Highers if:**

- You're applying for a competitive course at one of the four ancient universities and they ask for AHs in conditions
- You're applying to English universities, where Advanced Highers are weighted similarly to A Levels
- You want to skip first year ("direct entry to second year") at a Scottish university, which is allowed for some courses if you have AAA at Advanced Higher in relevant subjects

**You don't need Advanced Highers if:**

- You already have an unconditional or low-condition offer from a Scottish university
- Your S5 Higher results are already at the level your chosen course requires
- You're heading to a university course that doesn't ask for them in its published requirements

S6 with Advanced Highers is the standard route for the most competitive students. S6 with crash Highers, college courses, or a Foundation Apprenticeship is just as valid for everyone else.

## How widening access changes the picture

If you qualify for [widening access](/widening-access), the grade requirements above are not the ones you should worry about. The University of Edinburgh's Medicine course, for example, has a standard offer of AAAAA but a SIMD20 / care experienced contextual offer that drops several grades. Glasgow does the same. So does Strathclyde, Dundee, and most others.

This is not theoretical. It's a real, published, used-every-year part of the system. If you live in a SIMD20 or SIMD40 postcode, are care experienced, or are first-generation, [check your eligibility on Pathfinder](/widening-access) before you write off a course because the standard grades feel out of reach.

## What to do next

1. **[Check entry requirements for your shortlist](/courses)** — pick 5 to 10 courses you're considering and look at the actual published Higher grades. This is much more useful than worrying about averages.
2. **Talk to your guidance teacher about S5 timetabling.** Are you on track to take 5 Highers? Are any of them harder to combine because of the column system?
3. **Take widening access seriously.** If you qualify, it changes the grade conversation completely.

The Higher year is intense, but the goal is not perfection — it's a strong, balanced set of results that match the entry requirements for the courses you actually want.
