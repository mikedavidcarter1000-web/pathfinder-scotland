There's a lazy version of careers advice that goes "AI is the future, so do Computing Science." It is not wrong, exactly — Computing Science is genuinely useful and Scotland needs more people who can write code. But it is dramatically incomplete, and following it without thinking will leave you less prepared for an AI economy than someone who took a wider mix.

This article makes the opposite argument: in an AI-augmented workplace, **breadth beats narrow specialisation**, and the ideal subject combination mixes STEM and humanities, not one or the other.

## Computing Science is useful but not sufficient

Let's start with the obvious. Computing Science at SQA Higher is one of the more directly useful subjects you can take. It introduces you to programming, data structures, software design, and computer systems. If you do nothing else with technology in your life, it gives you a basic vocabulary that 90 percent of adults don't have.

Take it. The argument here isn't against Computing Science.

The argument is that **AI tools have made coding much faster than it used to be**. The bottleneck in software development has shifted from "can you write the code" to "do you understand the problem well enough to ask for the right code." A junior developer in 2026 who only knows how to write code, but doesn't understand the business problem, the user, or the bigger picture, is competing with AI tools that write code faster than they can.

The developer who is most valuable in an AI-augmented workplace is the one who combines:

- Coding skills (Computing Science)
- Domain knowledge (the actual industry — finance, healthcare, education, climate, design, whatever)
- Communication skills (English, social subjects, languages)
- Numerical reasoning (Maths, statistics)
- Design thinking (creative subjects, art, design and manufacture)

Computing Science alone covers one of those five. The other four come from the rest of the curriculum.

## English and communication skills matter more, not less

This is the argument that surprises people. Surely, in an AI-driven world, AI does the writing — so what's the point of English?

Two things. First, **AI doesn't write well by itself.** It produces plausible drafts. The human who can read those drafts, spot what's missing, fix what's wrong, and shape them into something a real reader actually wants is doing the work that matters. That skill is taught in English class, and nowhere else.

Second, **prompting AI is itself a writing skill.** A good prompt is a clearly worded request with the right context, constraints, and goals. A bad prompt produces vague output. The difference between a junior employee who gets useful work out of AI and one who gets garbage is overwhelmingly the quality of their writing — their ability to define what they want, in clear, structured language. That is exactly what English Higher trains you to do.

Communication beyond writing matters too. Stakeholder conversations, client meetings, team alignment, content strategy, internal explanation. These are not going away. They are growing.

## Maths and statistics underpin AI literacy

You don't need to be a mathematician to work with AI. But you do need numerical literacy — the ability to read a chart, understand a percentage, spot a misleading correlation, and roughly grasp what "the model has 78 percent accuracy" actually means.

Maths Higher gives you that. Applications of Maths is a perfectly good substitute if pure Maths isn't your strength. Either way, you should leave school comfortable enough with numbers that you don't get bullied by them in your job later.

For students aiming for any data-adjacent career — and that's most modern careers, not just analyst roles — Higher Maths is close to essential. The big mistake students make at S4 / S5 is dropping Maths early because "I'm not going to do Maths at uni." You don't need to do Maths at uni. You do need to be able to work with numbers as an adult professional.

## Sciences teach experimental thinking

This is the bit nobody talks about, and it might be the most important. Sciences train a specific habit of mind: **forming a hypothesis, testing it, looking at the evidence, and updating your view.**

This habit is enormously valuable in any AI-related work. AI systems behave in ways that aren't always predictable. The skill of "I think this prompt will work, let me try it, the result wasn't what I expected, here's what I think is going wrong, let me test that" is exactly the scientific method, applied to a very different subject. People who took Biology, Chemistry, or Physics learn this habit early. People who didn't, often have to learn it from scratch as adults.

You don't need to become a research scientist. You do need to be able to think like one occasionally. Even one science taken to National 5 starts that habit.

## Creative subjects develop human skills AI struggles with

Art, Design and Manufacture, Drama, Music, Dance, Graphic Communication, Photography. These subjects teach you how to make something where there's no "correct answer." That's a different mode of thinking from the maths-and-science world, and it's the mode that AI is still notably weak at.

When you make a piece of art, you're constantly making judgements: this colour, not that one; this composition, not that one; this mood, not that one. You're trusting your eye and your taste. AI can generate a thousand variations of an image, but it can't tell you which one feels right. Only a person with an aesthetic sense can do that.

In an AI economy, the people who can make those "this not that" judgements are increasingly valuable. They're the ones the AI tools serve, not the other way round.

This applies far beyond traditional creative jobs. Marketing, advertising, product design, user experience design, brand strategy, film and TV production, game design, journalism, publishing — every one of these fields needs people who can decide what's good. Creative subjects are where you start learning that.

## Social subjects develop critical thinking about AI's societal impact

History, Geography, Modern Studies, Politics, Sociology, RMPS. These subjects teach you to think about systems, power, fairness, and how human societies change over time. They train you to ask: "Who benefits from this? Who is harmed? Whose voice is missing? What might go wrong?"

That kind of thinking is now essential for anyone working with or near AI. AI systems make decisions that affect people's lives — credit scores, hiring, healthcare, policing, content moderation. The people best placed to spot when those decisions are unfair or harmful are the ones who learned to ask social and ethical questions in school.

This isn't a niche concern. Companies are actively hiring for "AI ethics" and "responsible AI" roles, and university departments are filling with people whose background combines computer science with philosophy, law, history, or social science. The best preparation for those roles starts at S5, when you choose whether to keep a humanities subject alongside the sciences.

## The ideal combination

If you forced me to pick the most future-resilient set of S5 Highers, here's what I'd recommend:

1. **English** — non-negotiable
2. **Maths** (or Applications of Maths) — non-negotiable
3. **One science** (Biology is most flexible, Physics is hardest, Chemistry sits in the middle)
4. **One humanities** (History or Modern Studies for breadth, Geography for systems thinking)
5. **Computing Science** OR **a creative subject** OR **a modern language** — depending on where your interests lie

This isn't a magic formula. It's a default. The point is the shape: two anchors (English, Maths), one science, one humanities, and one wildcard. That mix gives you breadth across the things AI is bad at (judgement, communication, ethics, creativity) and the things it amplifies (analysis, code, structured thinking).

## The honest mistake to avoid

The mistake students and parents make is treating "AI is the future" as "everyone should become a software engineer." That's wrong on three levels.

1. **Software engineering is itself being changed by AI**, and demand for entry-level coders may actually fall as AI tools take more of that work.
2. **Most AI jobs aren't "AI jobs"** — they're regular jobs being done with AI assistance. Marketing roles, healthcare roles, finance roles, teaching roles. You don't need to be the one building the AI; you need to be the one using it well.
3. **The world needs more than coders.** It needs nurses, teachers, builders, designers, lawyers, vets, social workers, electricians, chefs, scientists, writers, and people who can do things AI can't.

If you're genuinely passionate about software engineering, study it. If you're not, don't pretend to be. Your most resilient future comes from doing what you're actually good at, in a field you care about, while learning to use AI tools to extend yourself.

## How to future-proof your subject choices

Three rules of thumb.

1. **Keep both wings of the curriculum.** At least one science and one humanities until S5, ideally both into S6.
2. **Take English and Maths seriously.** These are the two qualifications that show up in every entry requirement and underpin every adult career.
3. **Pick at least one subject that involves making something.** Computing, Art, Design and Manufacture, Music, Drama. The act of producing work and getting feedback is what builds judgement.

You can't predict the AI tools of 2035. You can prepare a mind that's flexible, articulate, numerate, scientific, and creative. The Scottish curriculum is well suited to that — the wide curricular areas exist precisely so you can keep options open. Use them.
