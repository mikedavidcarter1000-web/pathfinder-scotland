Taking a gap year is a common choice for Scottish school leavers, but the guidance available is almost entirely written for English students. Scotland has its own funding system, its own application timelines, and its own considerations. Here is what actually matters if you are thinking about deferring.

## Deferring your UCAS offer

If you have already applied through UCAS and received an offer, most Scottish universities allow you to defer for one year. You need to contact the university admissions office directly to request a deferral -- do not assume it will happen automatically.

Some competitive courses (particularly Medicine, Dentistry, Veterinary Medicine and Nursing) may not allow deferrals, or may only allow them in exceptional circumstances. Check with the university before making plans.

If you have not yet applied, you can simply apply in the following UCAS cycle instead. There is no disadvantage to applying at 18 rather than 17 -- in fact, some universities view a year of experience positively.

## SAAS funding and your gap year

This is the most important practical point: **SAAS funding is not affected by taking a gap year.** You are still eligible for tuition fee payment, bursaries and loans when you start your course, regardless of whether you deferred or took a year out before applying.

However, you cannot receive SAAS funding during your gap year itself. SAAS only pays once you are enrolled on an eligible course.

If you are planning to work during your gap year, be aware that your earnings may affect your household income assessment for the following year. If you earn significantly more than usual, it could reduce your bursary entitlement. In practice, most gap year earnings from part-time or temporary work are unlikely to push you over the relevant thresholds, but it is worth being aware of. See the [SAAS funding guide](/blog/saas-funding-application-guide-scotland) for a full breakdown of the income bands.

## What to do during a gap year

The value of a gap year depends entirely on what you do with it. Universities and employers value structured experience over unstructured time off.

### Working

Working is the most common gap year activity, and the most practical. Any employment gives you transferable skills, workplace experience and savings. If you can find work related to your intended degree or career -- a lab assistant, a teaching support role, work in a law firm or an engineering company -- even better.

### Volunteering

Volunteering is valued by universities, particularly for courses with a caring or community focus (social work, teaching, nursing, medicine). Organisations like [Volunteer Scotland](https://www.volunteerscotland.net/), Project Scotland and local voluntary organisations offer structured placements.

### Travel

Travel can be valuable but is not necessary. If finances do not allow it, do not feel pressure. A gap year spent working locally and volunteering is just as valid as one spent abroad, and it leaves you in a stronger financial position when you start.

### Informal study

Taking an online course, reading widely in your intended subject, or doing a short college course shows universities that you are genuinely engaged with your field. This is especially useful if your gap year personal statement update needs to justify the time away.

## The gap year and widening access

If you are from a widening access background (SIMD 1 or 2, care-experienced, first generation), a gap year can feel riskier. There is a legitimate concern about losing momentum, losing contact with school-based support, and drifting away from the university plan.

If you are in this position, consider staying connected with your SHEP programme (LEAPS, FOCUS West, ASPIRENorth, LIFT OFF or Reach) during your gap year. They can continue to support you even after you leave school.

Also check whether the widening access support you qualified for at application stage is preserved under a deferral. Most universities guarantee that a contextual offer stands through to the deferred year -- but confirm in writing. See [Widening access on Pathfinder](/widening-access) for full eligibility information.

## When a gap year is a good idea

- You are genuinely unsure what you want to study and need time to explore your options.
- You want to earn money to support yourself more comfortably at university.
- You need a mental health break after an intense exam period.
- You have a specific opportunity -- a job offer, a placement or a project -- that will genuinely strengthen your application or career prospects.

## When a gap year might not be the right choice

- You have a clear plan and strong motivation to start.
- You are concerned about losing momentum or support.
- The course you want does not allow deferrals.
- You do not yet have a realistic plan for what you will do during the year.

There is no right or wrong answer. The key is to make it a deliberate choice, not a default one. Use the [Pathfinder careers tool](/careers) to help clarify your direction if you are unsure about your subject or career path -- that clarity might tell you whether a year out would genuinely help.

## First-generation students and the gap year

If you are the first in your family to apply to university, the concern about losing momentum during a gap year is real. Without family experience of university to draw on, it can be harder to reconnect with the application mindset a year later. If this describes you, read [First in Your Family Going to University in Scotland?](/blog/first-generation-university-scotland) before deciding. It covers the support systems available to you both now and when you eventually start.
