If you're choosing your subjects right now, you're choosing them in a year that future historians will write about. AI tools went from "interesting experiments" in 2022 to "actively used by hundreds of millions of workers" by 2026. That change is real, and it does change the calculation about which careers are most resilient.

This guide is honest, balanced, and avoids the two extremes — neither "AI will automate everything by 2030" nor "AI is just a fad". Both are wrong. The truth is in the middle, and the middle is more useful for your decisions.

## What AI can do well in 2026

By the time you're reading this, AI tools can reliably do the following at near-human or above-human level:

- **Generate text** — articles, emails, summaries, code documentation, marketing copy
- **Write and debug code** — particularly for well-defined problems and standard frameworks
- **Analyse data** — clean spreadsheets, find patterns, produce charts, explain trends
- **Translate languages** — accurately for most common language pairs
- **Summarise documents** — turn 100 pages into a 1-page brief, often more accurately than a human
- **Answer customer questions** — for routine queries with predictable answers
- **Generate and edit images** — for design, marketing, illustrations
- **Tutor and explain concepts** — at all levels of difficulty, on demand, 24 hours a day
- **Interview transcription and meeting notes** — fast, cheap, consistent

These are not future capabilities. They are current ones. They're being used in workplaces every day, and the trend is towards more, not less.

## What AI cannot do well in 2026

Equally important — and equally honest:

- **Physical work in unstructured environments.** Plumbing, electrical work, joinery, building, healthcare touch work, hairdressing, agriculture, fishing, social care, dentistry, surgery. Anything where a robot would have to navigate a real, messy human environment.
- **Genuine emotional connection.** Counselling, therapy, social work, palliative care, teaching young children, ministry. AI can simulate empathy. Real humans know the difference at moments that matter.
- **Creative judgement under ambiguity.** Saying "this is good" or "this isn't right yet" in fields where the rules aren't fixed. Senior creative roles in art, design, music, architecture, film, journalism.
- **Trust-bearing roles.** Doctors, lawyers, accountants who sign off on something with their professional name and licence on the line. AI can draft. A regulated human still has to approve.
- **Physical presence and authority.** Teachers in a classroom, managers in a workplace, performers on a stage. The room changes when a real person is in it.
- **Genuinely novel research.** Not "look up existing answers" but "discover something nobody has worked out yet." AI helps. The breakthrough still requires the human.
- **Negotiation between humans with conflicting interests.** Mediation, diplomacy, complex sales, trade union work, M&A.

The pattern is consistent: AI is good at language and pattern tasks, weak at physical and trust tasks. It's good at speed and breadth, weak at depth and accountability.

## Which sectors are most affected

The most rigorous published research on this is the [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/news/the-anthropic-economic-index), which tracks how Claude users are actually using AI in real work. It shows the heaviest current AI adoption in:

- **Software development** — by far the largest single use case
- **Writing, editing, marketing copy, and content production**
- **Data analysis and reporting**
- **Customer service and support**
- **Research, summarisation, and knowledge work**
- **Education and tutoring**

The [University of Pennsylvania / OpenAI study on LLM exposure](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.10130) (Eloundou et al., 2023) found that around 80 percent of US workers had at least 10 percent of their tasks affected by current language models, and about 19 percent had over half their tasks affected. The most exposed occupations were in white-collar information work — particularly writing, analysis, programming, and routine office tasks. The least exposed were in physical, manual, and high-trust roles.

[McKinsey's 2023 generative AI report](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/mckinsey-digital/our-insights/the-economic-potential-of-generative-ai-the-next-productivity-frontier) similarly estimated that 60 to 70 percent of employee time across sectors could in principle be automated by AI. The same report stressed that "automation potential" doesn't equal "automation reality" — the gap between what AI can do and what economies actually decide to automate is large.

## Which sectors are least affected

Roles that require physical presence, regulated trust, deep human connection, or creative judgement are far less exposed:

- **Healthcare delivery** (nurses, doctors at the patient interface, physiotherapists, dentists)
- **Skilled trades** (electricians, plumbers, joiners, builders, mechanics)
- **Early years and primary teaching**
- **Social work, counselling, mental health**
- **Hospitality and front-of-house roles** in customer-facing settings
- **Personal services** (hairdressing, beauty, fitness training)
- **Engineering site work and construction management**
- **Police, fire, ambulance, military**
- **Judges, senior legal, regulatory roles**

These aren't "safe forever" — every sector will be affected at the edges — but they're the ones least at risk of being displaced wholesale.

## The single most important message

It is this: **AI augments most jobs rather than replacing them.**

The historical pattern with general-purpose technologies (electricity, the internet, the personal computer) is that they make existing workers more productive much more often than they eliminate jobs entirely. The jobs that disappear are usually narrow, repetitive ones. The jobs that survive change shape — they get more interesting, more leveraged, and often better paid.

A doctor with AI is a better doctor than one without. A teacher with AI lesson-planning tools spends more time on individual students. A social worker with AI admin tools spends more time on people. A lawyer with AI-drafted contracts spends more time on judgement and strategy.

The mistake students make is assuming "AI is good at X, therefore I shouldn't go into X." The right framing is: **AI is good at X, therefore the people who go into X will be much more leveraged if they learn to use it well.**

## Skills that matter more, not less

In an AI-augmented economy, certain skills become more valuable, not less:

- **Critical thinking.** AI generates plausible-sounding answers that are sometimes wrong. The ability to spot when something is wrong matters more than ever.
- **Creativity and original thinking.** Defining the right problem is harder than solving a known one — and AI doesn't define problems for you.
- **Communication.** The ability to explain a complex idea to a non-expert is the bottleneck for almost every interesting job.
- **Adaptability.** Tools change every six months. The ability to learn a new tool quickly is itself a meta-skill.
- **Working with AI.** Knowing how to prompt, when to trust AI output, when to override it, and how to spot its errors is its own discipline now.
- **Domain expertise.** AI is a generalist. Combining AI with deep specialist knowledge in a field — medicine, law, engineering, teaching — is where the highest-leverage roles will be.
- **Human relationships.** Selling, leading, mentoring, persuading. The bits that aren't text on a screen.

## Why breadth in S3 matters more in an AI economy

If you knew exactly what jobs would exist in 2040, you could optimise your subjects for them. Nobody knows. The honest answer is that the world your future career will be lived in is fundamentally uncertain.

In an uncertain world, the right strategy is breadth. The S3 student who keeps both sciences and arts has more options than the one who specialised early. The S5 student who took both Maths and English Literature to Higher has a more flexible future than the one who only took STEM. The S6 student who took an Advanced Higher in History alongside Computing Science is exactly the kind of student a 2030 employer is looking for — someone who can think and code.

Don't optimise for the world as it is now. Optimise for adaptability in the world that's coming.

## What this means for subject choices

The practical takeaways:

- **Don't drop English.** The single most important skill in an AI-augmented workplace is the ability to write clearly and read critically. English underpins both.
- **Don't drop Maths.** Statistics, data, and logical reasoning underpin every AI tool. Even if you never write code, you need numerical fluency.
- **Keep at least one science.** Sciences teach experimental thinking — the discipline of testing whether something is actually true rather than whether it sounds true.
- **Take Computing if you can.** Not because every job will be coding, but because understanding what code is and how it works gives you a huge head start in working with AI tools.
- **Don't drop the humanities and arts entirely.** Critical thinking, creativity, and human judgement are precisely the skills AI doesn't have. Studying them is studying the human edge.
- **A modern language is still valuable.** AI can translate, but speaking another language gives you cultural fluency, not just word-for-word translation.
