A **crash Higher** means studying a Higher in a subject you didn't take at National 5. You skip the N5 and go directly to Higher, usually in S5 or S6.

It's a useful tactic in the right subject. It's a disaster in the wrong one.

## Good subjects to crash

These subjects are designed in a way that doesn't strictly require the N5 content as a prerequisite:

- **Psychology** — by far the most commonly crashed subject. Standalone course, no N5 dependency.
- **Philosophy**
- **Sociology**
- **Politics**
- **RMPS**
- **Modern Studies**

Most students crashing Psychology in S6 do well, particularly if they've done strong essay-based subjects already.

## Possible but harder

These are crashable but you'll be doing more catch-up work than your classmates:

- History
- English
- Geography
- Business Management
- Art and Design
- PE

The challenge is usually the volume of background reading or technique that N5 students built up over the previous year.

## Not recommended

These subjects build directly on the N5 syllabus and crashing them is genuinely difficult:

- **Mathematics** — every Higher topic builds on N5
- **Chemistry**
- **Physics**
- **Biology**
- **Modern Languages** — you need years of vocabulary
- **Computing Science** — programming and theory both require N5 base
- **Music** — practical and theory both depend on prior work

A crash Higher in any of these is realistic only for an exceptional student with a closely related background (a Maths whizz crashing Physics, for example).

## School policies vary

There's no national rule on crash Highers. School policies range widely:

- **Lenzie Academy** — prohibits crash Highers in S5.
- **James Gillespie's** (Edinburgh) — permits them only with department head permission.
- **Most Edinburgh schools** — allow them in S6 case-by-case.

Talk to your guidance teacher early. If your school's policy is restrictive, you may have more flexibility in S6.

## How to succeed at a crash Higher

If you've decided to crash a subject, the difference between a pass and a fail usually comes down to preparation:

1. **Get the N5 textbook anyway.** Read it over the summer before S5/S6 starts.
2. **Start early.** The first few weeks matter more than they do for any other subject.
3. **Talk to the teacher on day one.** Make sure they know you're crashing — most are happy to point you at the gaps.
4. **Use Qualifications Scotland (formerly SQA) past papers early.** Don't wait until prelim time. Try one in October.
5. **Be honest with yourself by October.** If you're already adrift, you can usually drop without it counting against you.

## Universities don't care

Universities see the **grade**, not how you got there. The qualification is identical to a Higher taken with the N5 underneath it. There is no penalty, no asterisk, no note on your record.

This is genuinely true even for the most competitive courses. Edinburgh Medicine doesn't care whether you took Higher Chemistry with or without N5 — they care that you got an A.

## Sometimes the slower route is smarter

Crashing isn't always the right choice. If you don't need the grade for your S5 UCAS application, **N5 in S5 then Higher in S6** is often the smarter plan:

- You get the grounding you'd otherwise be missing.
- You have a fallback (the N5) if life gets in the way.
- Your Higher pass rate is much higher with the N5 underneath it.

Crashing is most useful when **you need the Higher for an S5-results application** and there's no slower route available.

## What to do next

1. **Check the school policy.** Don't assume it's allowed.
2. **Pick from the "good to crash" list** unless you have a strong reason not to.
3. **Decide by August.** Crashing decided in October is much harder than crashing planned over the summer.
