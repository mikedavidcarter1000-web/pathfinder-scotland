**SIMD** stands for the **Scottish Index of Multiple Deprivation**. It divides Scotland into 6,976 small geographical areas called *datazones* and ranks each one across seven measures of disadvantage. The result is a national map of where deprivation is concentrated.

Universities use SIMD to make sure they admit students from across Scotland — not just the affluent suburbs. If your postcode falls into the most deprived 20 percent (SIMD20) or 40 percent (SIMD40) of areas, you may qualify for **lower entry requirements**, **guaranteed offers**, and additional support.

This guide explains what SIMD is, how to check yours, and exactly what each Scottish university does with the information.

## How to check your SIMD score

Two ways:

1. The [Scottish Government's official postcode lookup tool](https://www.gov.scot/collections/scottish-index-of-multiple-deprivation-2020/).
2. Pathfinder's [widening access check](/widening-access) — enter your postcode and we look up your SIMD decile automatically.

The result is a number between 1 and 10 (the *decile*). Decile 1 is the most deprived 10 percent of areas in Scotland. Decile 10 is the least deprived. SIMD20 means deciles 1 and 2. SIMD40 means deciles 1 to 4.

It's based on **where you live**, not on your personal circumstances. You don't need to provide bank statements or a hardship case.

## What universities actually do with SIMD20 status

Every Scottish university uses SIMD20 to lower entry requirements. The exact wording varies, but the effect is the same: a student from a SIMD20 postcode gets a lower offer than the standard one.

| University | SIMD20 offer (sample) |
|------------|------------------------|
| **Aberdeen** | Guaranteed unconditional at BBC |
| **Glasgow** | Guaranteed BBBB with Top-Up / Summer School completion |
| **Edinburgh** | Plus Flag — offers reduced by 1 to 2 grades |
| **St Andrews** | Gateway programme at BBBB |
| **Dundee** | Access Summer School: BBBC to BBBB |
| **Heriot-Watt** | Guaranteed BBBC |
| **Stirling** | Up to 2-grade reduction |
| **Glasgow Caledonian** | Minimum BCCC to CCC |
| **University of the West of Scotland** | Entry at CCC, or CC plus a widening access programme |

These are sample offers. Specific courses (Medicine, Dentistry, Veterinary) may still require higher grades — check each course's entry profile on its university website or in our [course search](/courses).

Most universities also add:

- **A guaranteed interview** for courses that interview as part of the selection process
- **Additional support** — mentoring, dedicated bursaries, named contact people, priority access to halls of residence

## SIMD40 — the extended version

A few universities extend their widening access provisions to SIMD40 (the most deprived 40 percent of areas). This widens the pool considerably.

The clearest examples:

- **Scottish Rural College (SRUC)** for agriculture, animal science, and rural land use
- **University of the West of Scotland** for several programmes
- **Selected programmes at the University of Glasgow** in less competitive subjects

Eligibility lists change — always check with the specific university.

## Other widening access indicators beyond SIMD

SIMD is the most commonly used indicator, but it's not the only one. Several other circumstances trigger widening access entitlements at Scottish universities.

- **Care-experienced** — every Scottish university guarantees an offer to a care-experienced applicant who meets the minimum entry requirements
- **Young carers** — recognised by all major Scottish universities, with named programmes at Edinburgh, Glasgow, and others
- **Estranged students** — those without family support
- **SHEP school attendance** — Scottish Higher Education Programme schools, identified annually
- **First in family to attend university** — specific programmes at Aberdeen, Dundee, Stirling
- **Refugees and asylum seekers** — additional entitlements via the Sanctuary Scholarship scheme
- **Armed forces families** — recognised at most universities

You can hold more than one of these indicators at the same time. Multiple indicators strengthen your case — they're additive, not alternative.

## The 7 SIMD domains, explained

SIMD is built from seven separate measures of disadvantage. The composite ranking is the average across them, but each one has a story of its own.

1. **Income** — proportion of people on income-related benefits.
2. **Employment** — proportion of working-age people who are unemployed or unable to work.
3. **Education** — qualifications, school attendance, school leaver destinations.
4. **Health** — life expectancy, hospital admissions, prescriptions for mental health conditions.
5. **Access to services** — drive times to GP, school, post office, supermarket. **This is why rural areas often score badly even if income is reasonable.**
6. **Crime** — recorded crime rates.
7. **Housing** — overcrowding, lack of central heating.

The composite is the headline number. The component breakdown explains *why* a particular postcode scores where it does. Two SIMD20 postcodes might have very different stories — one urban-deprivation, one rural-isolation — but they're treated the same by university widening access teams.

## Common questions answered

**Is it unfair on students from "non-deprived" areas?**

No. The research is clear: students entering on contextual offers based on SIMD perform just as well at university as standard-offer students. The Commissioner for Fair Access reports show parity in degree outcomes between the two groups. The system corrects for unequal opportunities at school, not for ability.

**Does my SIMD score appear on my UCAS form?**

Not directly — but UCAS shares your postcode with universities, and the universities run the SIMD lookup themselves. You don't need to do anything special. Your postcode does the work for you.

**I just moved house — which postcode applies?**

The address you give on your UCAS form. If you've recently moved out of a SIMD20 area, talk to the university — most will use the postcode you've lived at longest during your school years.

**Can I combine SIMD with other indicators?**

Yes. SIMD20 + first-in-family + care-experienced is treated as a stronger widening access case than SIMD20 alone. Universities are explicit about this.

**Does SIMD affect college entry?**

Not directly — colleges generally have lower entry requirements anyway. But it can affect bursary entitlements and hardship funds at college.

If your postcode is in SIMD20, you have access to lower entry requirements at every Scottish university. This isn't charity. It's recognition that grades don't exist in a vacuum, and that someone who got BBB in a school where the average was CCC has worked harder than someone who got AAA in a school where the average was AAA. Use what's available to you.
