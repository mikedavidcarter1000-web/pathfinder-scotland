If nobody in your family has been to university, the whole process can feel like it was designed for people who already know how it works. The application forms, the jargon, the assumption that someone at home can explain what a personal statement is -- it can be isolating.

But here is the thing: Scotland has one of the most supportive systems in the world for students who are first in their family to go to university. Free tuition, non-repayable bursaries, adjusted entry requirements, and dedicated support programmes all exist specifically because the Scottish Government and Scottish universities recognise that talent is everywhere, but opportunity is not.

This guide brings all of that together in one place.

## What "first generation" actually means

Most Scottish universities define a first-generation student as someone whose parents or guardians have not completed a degree-level qualification. Some institutions extend this to include guardians who did not attend university at all, even if they did not complete a degree.

You do not need to prove this formally at application stage. UCAS asks about parental education and occupation, and universities use this alongside your postcode (via the Scottish Index of Multiple Deprivation, or SIMD) and your school's historical progression data to identify applicants who may benefit from contextualised admissions.

## Free tuition -- this is not a myth

If you are a Scottish-domiciled student studying at a Scottish university, the Student Awards Agency Scotland (SAAS) pays your tuition fees directly to the university. You do not pay upfront, you do not take out a tuition fee loan, and there is nothing to repay. This applies to undergraduate degrees of up to five years (including integrated Masters programmes).

This is worth around £1,820 per year -- paid by SAAS, not by you.

## Bursaries you may be entitled to

SAAS also provides living cost support. If your household income is below £20,999, you are entitled to a Young Students' Bursary of £2,000 per year (non-repayable) plus a maintenance loan. Even at higher income bands, bursary support is available up to £33,999 household income.

On top of the SAAS bursary, individual universities offer their own support:

- The **Robertson Trust Scholarship** provides up to £4,250 per year for the full duration of an undergraduate degree, targeting students from the most disadvantaged backgrounds. It is nomination-based -- your school, college or support organisation puts you forward.
- The **Unite Foundation** offers free year-round accommodation (365 days) at partner universities for care-experienced and estranged students. In Scotland, partner institutions include the University of Edinburgh, Robert Gordon University and the University of Strathclyde.
- Many universities have hardship and discretionary funds available throughout the year. These do not need to be applied for in advance -- you can access them if your circumstances change.

## Contextual admissions -- what they mean for you

Every Scottish university operates a contextual admissions policy. This means they look at your application in the context of your background, not just your grades.

If you live in a postcode classified as SIMD 1 or SIMD 2 (the 20 per cent most deprived areas in Scotland), or if you attend a school with low university progression rates, or if you are first in your family, universities will consider you against minimum entry requirements rather than standard ones. The difference can be significant -- at St Andrews, the standard offer might be AAAAB at Higher, while the Gateway entry route requires BBBB.

At Edinburgh, Strathclyde, Glasgow and most other Scottish universities, the gap between standard and minimum offers is typically one to two grades per subject.

## Widening access programmes -- your regional support network

Scotland runs several regional widening access programmes, collectively known as Schools for Higher Education Programme (SHEP) partnerships:

- **LEAPS** covers south-east Scotland (Edinburgh, Lothians, Borders, Fife). If your school is a LEAPS-eligible school, you get access to summer schools, mentoring, campus visits and application support.
- **FOCUS West** covers the west of Scotland (Glasgow, Lanarkshire, Renfrewshire, Ayrshire, Dumbarton). It runs a structured programme from S4 onward, including residential summer schools at partner universities.
- **ASPIRENorth** covers the north-east (Aberdeen, Aberdeenshire, Moray). It partners with the University of Aberdeen and Robert Gordon University.
- **LIFT OFF** covers Tayside (Dundee, Angus, Perth and Kinross), working with the University of Dundee and Abertay University.
- **Reach** covers the Highlands and Islands, working with UHI.

Ask your guidance teacher whether your school is part of a SHEP programme. If it is, there is dedicated support available that most students never hear about.

## The college route -- do not overlook it

If your grades are not where you need them to be, or if you want a more gradual transition into higher education, the college-to-university articulation route is one of Scotland's best-kept secrets.

You can study an HNC (one year) or HND (two years) at a local college, then transfer into year two or year three of a degree at a partner university. This means you can still graduate with the same degree as someone who entered directly from school -- it just takes a different path. Read the full guide: [From College to University in Scotland](/blog/college-to-university-articulation-scotland).

College tuition is also free, and you may be eligible for an FE bursary of up to £125.55 per week to cover living costs.

## Practical steps if you are in S4, S5 or S6 right now

First, make sure your subject choices keep your options open. Some university courses have mandatory subject requirements -- if you drop Higher Maths or a science in S5, certain doors close. Use the [Pathfinder simulator](/simulator) to check which courses your current subjects qualify you for.

Second, talk to your guidance teacher about SHEP programmes and contextualised admissions. They can flag your application as eligible for adjusted offers.

Third, apply for SAAS funding as early as possible. Applications open in April each year, and the guarantee deadline is 30 June. If you apply by then, your funding should be in place before September.

Fourth, do not let anyone -- including yourself -- talk you out of it. Scotland's system is specifically designed to make university accessible for people from your background. The money is there. The support is there. The adjusted offers are there. The only thing missing might be someone telling you it is possible.

It is.
