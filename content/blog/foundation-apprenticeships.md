If you've never heard of a Foundation Apprenticeship, you're not alone. Most Scottish students leave school without ever being told they exist. That's a shame, because for the right student, a Foundation Apprenticeship is one of the most useful things you can do in S5 and S6 — and it counts as one of your Higher-level qualifications for university entry.

This guide explains what they are, how they work in practice, and how to decide whether one is right for you.

## What is a Foundation Apprenticeship?

A Foundation Apprenticeship (FA) is a two-year work-based qualification you take alongside your school subjects in S5 and S6. It's run jointly by your school, a local college, and an employer or group of employers. It sits at SCQF Level 6 — the same level as a Higher.

That last point is the one most students don't know. **A completed Foundation Apprenticeship counts as one of your five S5 / S6 Higher-level qualifications for university entry.** Every Scottish university accepts FAs as part of their entry requirements. Several actively prefer applicants who have one, because it shows real workplace experience alongside academic study.

Foundation Apprenticeships are funded by [Skills Development Scotland](https://www.apprenticeships.scot/become-an-apprentice/foundation-apprenticeships/) and delivered by local colleges in partnership with schools and employers. There's no cost to you.

## Which frameworks are available?

The current frameworks vary slightly by region, but the main ones include:

- **Business Skills**
- **Children and Young People** — leads into childcare, primary teaching, social work
- **Civil Engineering**
- **Creative and Digital Media**
- **Engineering**
- **Financial Services**
- **Food and Drink Technologies**
- **Hardware and System Support / IT**
- **Software Development**
- **Scientific Technologies** — laboratory science
- **Social Services and Healthcare**
- **Accountancy**

Not every framework is available in every region. The full list and local availability is at [apprenticeships.scot](https://www.apprenticeships.scot/become-an-apprentice/foundation-apprenticeships/), or check with your school's careers adviser. In the Edinburgh area, [Edinburgh College runs FAs in several frameworks](https://www.edinburghcollege.ac.uk/courses/school-college-partnership) in partnership with local secondary schools.

## How it works in practice

This is the bit nobody explains properly. Here's a typical week for an S5 student doing a Foundation Apprenticeship:

- **Monday, Wednesday, Friday** — normal school timetable
- **Tuesday and Thursday afternoons** — at your local college doing the FA programme

You leave school after lunch on those days and head to college, often on a school-arranged bus or train. At college you do the academic component of the FA — coursework, units, group projects.

Across the two years, you also complete around 80 hours of workplace experience with a local employer. This isn't a one-off "work experience week" — it's a recurring placement, often half a day a week or longer blocks during school holidays.

Because the FA replaces one of your subject choices in S5 and S6, your weekly timetable still adds up to a full week. You're not doing extra work — you're doing different work.

## How universities view Foundation Apprenticeships

Every Scottish university accepts FAs as part of an offer. They're treated as equivalent to a Higher in tariff terms. The key thing to understand is that universities count them as **one** of your subjects, not as an extra. So if you do four Highers and an FA, you have five qualifications. If you do five Highers and an FA, you have six.

For some courses — especially vocational or applied courses like Engineering, Civil Engineering, Computer Science, Nursing, and Social Work — universities actively rate FA applicants more highly because they bring real workplace context to the degree. The University of the West of Scotland, Edinburgh Napier, and Glasgow Caledonian have all run open recruitment campaigns specifically targeting FA students.

For the most academically traditional courses (Medicine, Dentistry, Law at the ancient universities) the FA carries the same tariff weight but isn't actively preferred. The honest summary: an FA never hurts your application. For some courses, it actively helps.

## Foundation vs Modern vs Graduate Apprenticeships

This bit confuses everyone, so here's the short version.

| Type | Stage | Where you are | Outcome |
|------|-------|---------------|---------|
| Foundation Apprenticeship | S5 / S6 | Still at school | SCQF 6 — counts towards UCAS / uni entry |
| Modern Apprenticeship | After school | Working full-time, training on the side | Industry qualification, paid job |
| Graduate Apprenticeship | After school | At university doing a degree, employer pays | Honours degree + work experience |

Foundation Apprenticeships are the school-stage version. Modern Apprenticeships are the post-school full-time work option. Graduate Apprenticeships are the degree-while-working option (paid by the employer, no tuition fees, no SAAS forms — covered in our [cost of university guide](/blog/cost-of-university-scotland)).

## Who is a Foundation Apprenticeship right for?

**If you learn better by doing.** FAs are practical and applied. You spend time in workplaces, with real adults doing real jobs, working on real problems. If sitting in a classroom for six hours a day grinds you down, an FA replaces a chunk of that with hands-on work.

**If you're unsure about full-time university.** An FA gives you a taste of professional life before you commit four years to a degree. Several students each year decide after their FA that they want to go straight into work or into a Modern Apprenticeship instead.

**If you want a competitive edge for vocational degrees.** If you're already aiming for an Engineering, Computer Science, Childcare, Nursing, or Social Work degree, the FA in the relevant framework is essentially a head start on year one of the degree.

**If you want experience to put on a CV.** An FA is much stronger evidence of work readiness than the standard "I worked Saturdays at Boots" personal statement line. It demonstrates actual professional experience to admissions tutors and to employers.

## Who is a Foundation Apprenticeship not right for?

**If you're absolutely committed to a research-heavy degree** (Medicine, Theoretical Physics, ancient History at St Andrews) the time you spend on the FA is time you could spend on a fifth or sixth Higher in a relevant subject. The FA still counts, but the marginal Higher might serve you better.

**If your school doesn't have transport sorted.** The college afternoons depend on your school being able to get you to and from the college campus. In rural areas this can be a real practical barrier — check the logistics before you commit.

**If you don't enjoy the framework area.** The FA is two years long and includes real workplace placement. If you're picking it just because it sounds prestigious, you'll have a miserable time.

## How to apply

1. **Talk to your school's career adviser or guidance teacher in S3 or S4.** Applications usually open in autumn of S4, with placements starting in S5.
2. **Check which frameworks your local college runs.** Edinburgh College, Fife College, City of Glasgow College, Forth Valley College, and others all have specific local lists.
3. **Apply early.** Popular frameworks (Software Development, Engineering, Children and Young People) fill up. Late applications are accepted if there's space, but it's not guaranteed.
4. **Tell your school you want to take one.** Some schools are more proactive about FAs than others. If yours hasn't mentioned them, raise it yourself.
