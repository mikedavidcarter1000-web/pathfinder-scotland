Every year, thousands of Scottish pupils search for "easiest Highers" and "hardest Highers" before making their subject choices. It is a reasonable question -- but the answer is more complicated than a simple ranking.

Pass rates vary significantly between subjects, and the published SQA statistics tell you something real. But they do not tell you what is easy or hard for you personally. Here is what the data actually shows, and how to use it.

## What the SQA pass rate data shows

SQA publishes pass rates (grade A to C) for every Higher subject annually. These figures include all candidates sitting the exam in a given year. Across recent exam diets, some clear patterns emerge.

Subjects with consistently **high pass rates** (above 80 per cent A to C) tend to include Modern Studies, Psychology, Philosophy, Music, Art and Design, Drama, Physical Education, and several technology subjects. These are not "easy" subjects -- they are subjects where the assessment structure, the type of student who chooses them, and the teaching approach tend to produce high completion rates.

Subjects with **lower pass rates** (below 70 per cent A to C in some years) include Mathematics, Physics, Chemistry and some modern languages. Again, this does not mean these subjects are impossible -- it reflects the fact that they have demanding content, many pupils take them because they are required rather than because they enjoy them, and the jump from National 5 to Higher is steep in quantitative subjects.

SQA publishes the full statistical data at [sqa.org.uk](https://www.sqa.org.uk/sqa/48269.html) each autumn. Check there for the most recent figures before making decisions.

## Why pass rates can be misleading

There are several reasons why raw pass rates do not tell you which subject will be easiest for you.

### Self-selection bias

Self-selection bias is the biggest factor. Subjects like Music and Drama tend to be chosen by pupils who are already strong in those areas. Very few pupils take Higher Music unless they play an instrument and enjoy the subject. In contrast, Higher Maths is taken by a much broader range of pupils -- including many who need it for university entry but do not particularly enjoy mathematics. This pulls the pass rate down relative to the actual difficulty of the course.

### Cohort size

A subject with 500 candidates nationally will have a more volatile pass rate than one with 15,000 candidates. Small cohort subjects can swing by 10 percentage points year to year based on a handful of papers. Treating a single year's data as definitive for a rare subject is risky.

### School context

If your school has a strong Chemistry department with experienced teachers, your personal likelihood of passing Higher Chemistry is higher than the national average. The opposite is true if your school has had staff turnover or uses non-specialist teachers for certain subjects. Local data -- your own school's outcomes -- matters more than national averages.

## The National 5 to Higher jump

One genuinely useful data point is the progression rate from National 5 to Higher. Research consistently shows that pupils who achieve an A or B at National 5 have a strong chance of passing the corresponding Higher. But pupils who achieve a C at National 5 have a significantly lower progression rate -- in some subjects, fewer than 30 per cent of C-grade National 5 students go on to pass the Higher.

This matters more than any national pass rate. If you got a C in National 5 Maths, taking Higher Maths is a genuine risk. If you got an A, it is a reasonable next step. Your own grade history is a better predictor than any published league table.

## What about crash Highers?

A "crash Higher" means taking a Higher without having studied the corresponding National 5. Schools vary widely in whether they allow this. In some subjects -- particularly social sciences and humanities -- it is feasible because the Higher course introduces enough new content that a motivated student can succeed. In sciences and mathematics, crash Highers are significantly harder because the content builds directly on National 5 knowledge.

If you are considering a crash Higher, talk to the subject teacher honestly about whether they think it is realistic for you. Some schools actively discourage it; others are more flexible. See also: [What is a crash Higher?](/blog/what-is-a-crash-higher)

## How to use this information

Do not choose subjects purely because they have high pass rates. Choose subjects you are interested in, that align with your potential career or university plans, and that you have a realistic chance of succeeding in based on your own track record.

Use the [Pathfinder Scotland subjects database](/subjects) to see what each Higher covers and which university courses it counts towards.

Use the [Pathfinder simulator](/simulator) to check which university courses your subject combination opens up. Some combinations -- particularly dropping all sciences, or avoiding Mathematics -- close more doors than people realise.

If you are torn between two subjects, consider the one where you have the stronger National 5 foundation. A Higher B in a subject you enjoy is worth more than a Higher D in a subject you chose because someone told you it was "easier."

## The right question to ask

Instead of "which Higher is easiest?", ask:

1. Do I have a solid National 5 grade in this subject (B or above)?
2. Do I have a genuine interest in the content, or will I find it a slog?
3. Does my school have strong teaching in this subject?
4. Does this subject open the university doors I need, or close them?
