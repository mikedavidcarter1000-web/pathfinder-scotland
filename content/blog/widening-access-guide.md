If you live in a less advantaged part of Scotland, are care experienced, are a young carer, or would be the first in your family to go to university, the door to higher education is open wider than you might think. Scotland's widening access programmes mean you may qualify for university with lower grades than the standard offer. This is not charity. It is recognition that not everyone starts from the same place.

This guide explains, in plain language, what widening access is, who qualifies, and how to check your eligibility on Pathfinder.

## What widening access actually means

Scottish universities are required by the [Scottish Funding Council](https://www.sfc.ac.uk/access-inclusion/widening-access/) to make sure their student body reflects the country they serve. Historically, students from poorer backgrounds, care experienced young people, and first-generation students were under-represented at university — particularly the older, more competitive ones. Widening access is the set of policies that fix this gap.

The idea is straightforward. If two students apply for the same course and one has had every academic advantage growing up while the other has not, treating them as if they started from the same place is unfair. Widening access adjusts the entry requirements to reflect that.

In practice this usually means a lower grade offer. A course with a standard offer of AAAB might offer AABB or BBBB to a student who qualifies. The course is exactly the same. The teaching is exactly the same. The degree at the end is exactly the same.

## SIMD — what it is and how to read it

SIMD stands for the [Scottish Index of Multiple Deprivation](https://www.gov.scot/collections/scottish-index-of-multiple-deprivation-2020/). It divides every postcode in Scotland into 10 groups, called deciles. Decile 1 is the most deprived 10 percent of areas. Decile 10 is the least deprived 10 percent.

Two phrases get used a lot: SIMD20 and SIMD40. SIMD20 means the most deprived 20 percent of areas — that's deciles 1 and 2. SIMD40 means the most deprived 40 percent — deciles 1 to 4.

Most Scottish universities offer reduced entry to SIMD20 students. Several go further and offer reduced entry to SIMD40. The University of Edinburgh, the University of Glasgow, and others publish detailed contextual offers for these groups. You can check which decile your postcode is in by entering it on Pathfinder — we look it up automatically using the SIMD 2020 dataset.

There is no shame in being in SIMD20 or SIMD40. It is a description of where you live, not a description of who you are or how clever you are. If your postcode qualifies, use it. The whole point of the system is that you should.

## Care experienced — the strongest entitlement

If you have ever been looked after by a local authority — at any age, for any length of time, in any kind of placement — you are care experienced. This includes kinship care, foster care, residential care, and being looked after at home with social work involvement. You don't need to have been in care recently, and you don't need to have been in care for long.

Every Scottish university offers a [Care Experienced Guarantee](https://www.celcis.org/knowledge-bank/spotlight/corporate-parenting/care-experienced-students). If you meet the minimum entry requirements for a course, you will be offered a place. That's the rule. It's the most generous widening access entitlement in Scotland and you don't need to do anything special to use it — you just declare your status when you apply through UCAS.

Care experienced students are also entitled to the [Care Experienced Bursary](https://www.saas.gov.uk/) — currently £9,000 per year while studying — which covers the full cost of living in most circumstances.

## Young carers

If you regularly care for a family member or friend who is ill, disabled, has a mental health condition, or struggles with addiction, you are a young carer. You don't need to live with them. You don't need to provide care every day. The definition is intentionally broad.

Young carers face real barriers to study and are recognised by Scottish universities as a widening access group. Several universities provide specific support, including the [Young Carers Bursary](https://www.saas.gov.uk/) and dedicated contact people to help you manage your studies alongside caring responsibilities.

## First generation students

If neither of your parents nor anyone else in your immediate family went to university, you are a first-generation student. This matters because students with no family experience of higher education face real informational disadvantages. They don't have anyone at home who can explain what UCAS is, or what halls are like, or how to write a personal statement.

Several Scottish universities recognise first-generation status as a widening access factor, often combined with other indicators.

## The named programmes

Beyond the contextual offer system, Scotland runs a number of named widening access programmes that you can apply to as a school student.

**[LEAPS](https://www.leapsonline.org/)** (Lothians Equal Access Programme for Schools). Runs in schools across the Lothians and works with the University of Edinburgh, Heriot-Watt, Edinburgh Napier, and Queen Margaret. Includes the Summer School, study skills sessions, and a guaranteed contextual offer at the partner universities for students who complete the programme.

**[FOCUS West](https://www.focuswest.org.uk/)**. Covers schools in the west of Scotland and works with the University of Glasgow and the University of Strathclyde. Includes the Top-Up programme and university summer schools.

**[REACH](https://reachscotland.com/)**. A national programme for students interested in Law, Medicine, Dentistry, and Veterinary Medicine — all of which are highly competitive. REACH runs at the four oldest Scottish universities and includes mentoring, residential events, and a guaranteed interview track.

**LIFT OFF** and **Top-Up**. Bridge programmes that run alongside the main university courses, helping students who qualify on widening access criteria adjust to university-level study.

**SWAP** (Scottish Wider Access Programme). This is for adult learners (usually 21 and over) who want to access university later in life — not directly relevant if you're in school, but worth knowing about for parents and family members.

## How universities use widening access data

The most common misunderstanding is that widening access means lower standards. It does not. Universities don't lower the standard of teaching. They don't lower the standard of the degree. They lower the entry grade only, and the evidence shows that students entering on contextual offers go on to do as well as everyone else.

A 2022 study by the [Commissioner for Fair Access](https://www.gov.scot/policies/universities/widening-access-to-university/) showed that contextual offer students at Scottish universities perform as well in their degrees as standard offer students. The grade gap at entry closes once everyone is on the same course.
