
Construction and Trades Careers - Executive Summary
The UK construction industry spans many occupations from unskilled labour to professional roles. Key trades include bricklayers, carpenters/joiners, electricians, plumbers, HVAC engineers, roofers, painters/decorators, plasterers, tilers, glaziers and specialists (e.g. asbestos removers, steel erectors), as well as heavy plant/crane operators. Professional roles cover site/safety management, surveying (quantity, land/building), estimating, civil engineering and facilities management. Each role has specific duties, skills and training routes (often NVQs/apprenticeships)
. Pay varies by trade and region - for example, electricians and plumbers typically start around GBP 24-26k and can rise to ~GBP 40-50k for experienced staff
. Career paths often run from apprentice to qualified tradesperson, then on to supervisory or specialist roles (e.g. team leader, estimator, foreman, or self-employed contractor)
. Demand drivers include new housing, infrastructure projects and energy retrofit programs. Health and safety are paramount: every role entails site hazards (e.g. heights for roofers, vibration for plant operators, dust/asbestos for demolition workers) requiring PPE and safety training. Most workers join unions or trade bodies (e.g. Unite, GMB, UCATT (now part of Unite), IOSH for H&S, CITB for training, CSCS for site access) to protect pay and conditions. Apprenticeships (2-4 years) remain the main entry route, often supplemented by CSCS/HSE courses, NVQs and short safety certifications
. Below are key points for each occupation, with representative sources.

Construction Labourer
Core tasks: General site support: moving materials, digging trenches, mixing concrete, maintaining site tidiness and assisting skilled trades
.
Daily activities: Aid bricklayers, carpenters etc.; site preparation (shoring trenches, laying out tools); operate hoisting gear; clear debris; basic demolition prep. Often rotating between tasks as needed.
Training: No formal qualifications needed. Entry via Level 1 construction courses or on-site training. Essential to pass the CITB Health, Safety & Environment test for operatives and obtain a CSCS labourer card
. Short courses in manual handling, first aid, power tools and working at height are recommended.
Skills/requirements: Good physical fitness, stamina and coordination
. Able to follow instructions and work as part of a team; basic numeracy for measurements. Must have good hearing/vision and health to work in variable conditions (often outdoors).
Tools: Hand tools (shovel, wheelbarrow, hand mixer, hammer, spirit level), basic power tools. No trade-specific tools required, but may operate small plant (jackhammers, mini excavators) under supervision.
Salary: ~GBP 18k-GBP 30k (starter to experienced). Pay tends to be higher in London/South East due to cost of living, often with extra for unsocial hours. (ONS data on labourers not readily available; use CITB/ashe estimates.)
Progression: After gaining experience, can move to skilled trade apprenticeship (e.g. bricklaying, plastering) or roles like Chargehand or Site Operative Supervisor. Some become plant operators or join specialist routes like groundworks.
Outlook: Steady demand in housebuilding and infrastructure. A key shortage role - often cited by CITB as critical. Demand follows construction booms.
Employers/Setting: All building sites (housing, commercial, civil). Common employers: construction contractors, housebuilders, civil engineering firms, local authorities. Work is almost entirely on site (urban, rural, roadworks, commercial).
H&S risks: Manual handling injuries, trips/falls, exposure to dust/cement, machinery accidents. PPE mandatory (boots, high-vis, gloves, helmets, eye/ear protection). Regular site inductions.
Hours/Conditions: Typically ~40 hours/week, with possible overtime or weekend work on busy sites. Seasonal exposure; outdoors in all weather. Work boots to practical clothing required. Usually unionised via Unite/GMB for pay negotiations.
Entry Tips: Start with a CSCS Labourer card (via CITB HS&E test). Gain experience on-site or take a Level 1 diploma. Essential: build reliability and show willingness to work hard. Short CITB courses (e.g. Health & Safety Awareness) help with card entry
. No academic prerequisites.
Bricklayer (Mason)
Core tasks: Build and repair walls, chimneys, pillars and other structures from brick, block and stone. Key duties include reading plans, measuring/site set-up, mixing mortar, laying bricks with trowel, cutting/setting bricks, pointing mortar joints, and constructing decorative heritage brickwork
.
Daily activities: Interpret architectural drawings; lay out building lines; build up brick courses in mortar; fill cavities with insulation; ensure verticality/level; prepare and repair mortar joints; clean and finish brickwork. Work often in a "gang" under a site supervisor.
Training: Usually via a 2-3 year bricklaying apprenticeship (e.g. NVQ Level 2 & 3) or college courses followed by on-the-job training. GCSEs (Maths, English) often required for apprenticeships. Additional NVQs in construction or bricklaying. CSCS Skilled Worker card required. Short courses in erecting scaffolding, first aid etc.
Skills/requirements: Excellent spatial reasoning, attention to detail and good eyesight (for straight courses). Physical strength and stamina (lifting blocks, kneeling). Coordination, balance (especially on scaffold) and patience. Basic math skills for setting out; teamwork ability
.
Tools: Trowels, brick hammers, chisels, spirit/laser level, tape measure, edging tools. At height: scaffolding/access equipment (harness). Hand held mixers and saws for cutting blocks
.
Salary: ~GBP 25k-GBP 45k (starter to experienced)
. Overtime and London/SE location premiums can raise earnings. Self-employed bricklayers charge higher day rates.
Progression: Move to Senior Mason/Foreman, Site Supervisor or Quantity Surveying roles. Specialise in heritage (stone masonry) or advanced skills (blockwork design). Many become contractors or run a self-employed bricklaying business
. Teaching apprentices or working as brickwork inspectors are long-term paths.
Outlook: Strong demand driven by residential construction and renovation. Shortage of bricklayers in UK (CITB and industry surveys highlight the skill gap). Green construction (e.g. insulation) creates niche work.
Employers/Setting: Employed by construction/housebuilding firms, bricklaying contractors, restoration specialists or government contractors. Work sites include new build housing, commercial buildings or heritage sites (museums, churches). Mix of indoor (partial) and outdoor.
H&S risks: Falls from scaffold, musculoskeletal injuries (repetitive motion), exposure to cement/dust (eye/skin irritation), heavy lifting. Requires safety boots, gloves, helmets, eye protection. At height, harness and guardrails. Follow HSE guidelines on brickwork.
Hours/Conditions: ~40-44h/week
; often Mon-Fri with some Saturday work possible. Early starts (for daylight). Seasonal outdoor exposure. Union/Association: Bricklayers' Union (UCATT part of Unite) often active. Professional affiliation: CITB for training standards.
Entry Tips: Gain a CSCS Skilled Worker card (need HS&E test and qualifications). Undertake an intermediate bricklaying apprenticeship (entry GCSEs usually required). Short "taster" courses or work shadowing can help. Good brickwork portfolios or practical tests can also impress employers
.
Carpenter / Joiner
Core tasks: Cut, shape and install wooden components (frames, doors, staircases, furniture, formwork). Carpenters work on structural elements (timber frames, roof trusses), while joiners typically focus on fine woodwork (joints, cabinetry). Tasks include measuring timber, cutting with saws, assembling/erecting structures, fitting parts and finishing surfaces.
Daily activities: Read drawings/layouts; measure and mark wood; cut/machine timber; fit panels, door frames, skirting, decking; assemble on-site or in workshop; use power and hand tools (saws, planes, nail guns); finish surfaces (sanding, varnish). Often rotate between workshop (joinery) and site (installation).
Training: Apprenticeship (Level 2 & 3) is standard. Entry needs practical shop/tech courses or T-Level in Construction. Carpentry/Joinery diplomas (NVQ) or college courses. Key skills: mathematics (for angles), drawing interpretation, woodworking certification. CSCS card needed. Additional short courses in CNC machinery or setting out.
Skills/requirements: Manual dexterity, physical strength, good hand-eye coordination. Patience, precision and spatial sense for complex joints. Hearing (woodworking noise), depth vision and good balance (work at height on scaffolds). Creativity and problem-solving for custom work.
Tools: Saws (circular, band, panel), chisels, drills, nail guns, planers, levels, routers. In joinery: lathes, routers, spray guns. Safety gear: ear defenders, masks for wood dust, safety glasses.
Salary: ~GBP 24k-GBP 40k. Site carpenters often earn more (due to hazard pay) than shop joiners. Regional premium for London and large projects.
Progression: Can become Site Carpentry Supervisor, Site Manager, or Estimator. Skilled joiners may move into bespoke furniture making or quality control. Self-employment as a carpenter or specialist joiner is common. Some progress to teaching carpentry or trade examiner roles.
Outlook: Ongoing demand in housing and commercial renovation. Green building (timber frames, energy-efficient retrofits) boosts work. Skilled carpenters are in short supply. Digital tools (CNC, BIM) introduce new opportunities.
Employers/Setting: Construction firms, timber frame companies, bespoke joinery shops. Work settings include construction sites (housing, schools, offices), workshops (furniture manufacturing) and sometimes domestic/heritage projects. Both indoor and outdoor work.
H&S risks: Cuts/impalement from tools, vibration injuries, falls (working on roofs or scaffolds), inhalation of wood dust. PPE: eye, ear, dust masks, cut-resistant gloves. Use push sticks, dust extraction, guardrails.
Hours/Conditions: ~40-45h/week (Mon-Fri), with overtime on project deadlines. Work can be at odd hours in commercial fit-outs. Industry bodies: CITB and Construction Industry Federation. Unions: Unite/CITB for courses.
Entry Tips: Start with a Level 1 woodcraft course or carpentry apprenticeship; get a CSCS card early. Gain basic woodworking skills (even DIY projects). Certified courses in power tool safety and first aid add value.
Electrician
Core tasks: Install, inspect and maintain electrical power systems. Work includes running cables, fitting sockets/fuses, wiring lighting, testing circuits, and installing control panels
. May specialise in domestic, commercial or industrial systems, or green tech (EV charging, solar inverters).
Daily activities: Read wiring diagrams; draw cable routes; fit trunking/conduit; connect switches, lighting and appliances; test systems (insulation resistance, continuity); repair faults; program control systems. Often on call for emergencies.
Training: Mandatory apprenticeship (Level 3) or technical qualification. Entry requires GCSEs (incl. maths, English). Must hold 18th Edition IET Wiring Regs certificate and Part P if domestic. Electrical NVQs/City & Guilds or AM2 assessments. CSCS card and ECS/Electrotechnical Certification Scheme card are needed. NIC EIC Certification for certain tasks. Short courses: working at height, PAT testing, multi-meter use.
Skills/requirements: Strong electrical/mechanical aptitude. Good eyesight and steady hand (fine wiring). Attention to safety (risk of shock). Problem-solving skills and maths (for calculations). Physically fit for climbing ladders; must pass an on-site health screening (no epilepsy/heart conditions).
Tools: Voltage testers, multimeter, cable strippers, crimping tools, drills, fish tape, socket sets, ladders/scaffolds. PPE: insulated gloves, boots, goggles. Common equipment: distribution boards, wiring accessories, conduits and meter boxes.
Salary: ~GBP 26k-GBP 45k
. City work or emergency call-outs boost earnings. Self-employed electricians can charge GBP 150-GBP 200/day. Region: London/Southeast typically 10-20% higher.
Progression: Senior Electrician -> Site Electrician -> Contracts Manager or Estimator. Specialisations: AV systems, building management, train signalling. Many gain electrical engineering degrees or obtain ECS Gold Card. Others start businesses (contracting).
Outlook: Ongoing demand (new builds, retrofits, renewable installations) means good prospects. Smart grid and EVs drive new roles. Skilled shortages are common. HSE emphasises safety.
Employers/Setting: Electrical contractors, maintenance teams, construction firms, public sector (hospitals, schools). Sites range from houses to high-rises, factories and offshore. Both indoor and outdoor (power lines). Office-based planning also occurs.
H&S risks: Electric shock, falls, working in confined/high locations, arc-flash. Strict compliance with BS 7671 wiring regs and PPE (RCDs, insulated tools, gloves). Regular H&S training (IOSH/NEBOSH).
Hours/Conditions: ~37-45h/week
, often Mon-Fri with some overtime. Rotational on-call shifts common in maintenance roles. Union: Unite/GMB. Professional: Institution of Engineering and Technology (IET).
Entry Tips: Complete a Level 3 apprenticeship or City & Guilds; register with JIB for ECS card. Early CSCS card and CITB H&S test. Building up basics (e.g. student wiring kits, DIY practice) is helpful. Short courses in PAT testing or 18th Ed regs accelerate entry.
Plumber / Gas/Heating Engineer
Core tasks: Install and service water, heating and gas systems. Work includes fitting pipes, valves, baths, sinks, boilers and radiators; commissioning gas/oil systems; maintaining vent systems; fixing leaks and blockages
.
Daily activities: Lay out pipework, solder/fit joints, install central heating, replace boiler parts, respond to plumbing emergencies (burst pipe, no hot water), test for compliance (gas tightness). May fit boilers/heat pumps or ventilations systems.
Training: Plumbing apprenticeship (Level 2-3). GCSEs in science, maths, English for entry. Complete ACS (Gas Safe) for gas engineers. NVQs in Plumbing and Heating. CITB HSE course plus CSCS card. Short courses: Leadwork, sanitation, renewables (solar thermal, heat pumps), flue systems.
Skills/requirements: Good practical skills; understanding of fluid dynamics and materials. Healthy, as work may be in cramped spaces. Must pass medical for gas work. Customer service (often residential calls).
Tools: Pipe wrenches, soldering torches, pipe cutters, pressing tools, threaders, plungers, safety valve spanners. Digital manometers. PPE: gloves (for chemicals/hot pipes), goggles for cutting/soldering.
Salary: ~GBP 24k-GBP 46k
. Specialized gas engineers earn more (with ACS certification). Overtime and emergency call-out pay up earnings. London rates higher.
Progression: Further training to Master Plumber, Gas Service Technician, or Heating Engineer. Can move into ventilation/air-conditioning. Many become Plumbing Supervisors, Estimators or start a contracting business. OFTEC oil certification or RIUK gas qualification are career boosts
.
Outlook: Strong demand (housing, refurbishment, green tech like heat pumps). Skills shortage, especially gas-certified plumbers. Continuous need for maintenance.
Employers/Setting: Plumbing contractors, construction companies, local authorities, housebuilder services. Buildings (homes, offices, schools, industrial) and outdoors (underground drainage). Work mostly indoors in wet conditions.
H&S risks: Slips on wet floors, burns (hot pipes), chemical exposure (solvents for drain cleaning). Gas leaks (carbon monoxide risk). PPE: gloves, eye protection, proper ventilation. Gas safeness paramount (Gas Safe regs).
Hours/Conditions: 37-45h/week
; often Monday-Friday with possible weekend/after-hours work for emergencies. Site or domestic settings at all hours. Unions: Unite/GMB. Key reg bodies: Gas Safe Register, Water Regulations.
Heating/Ventilation (HVAC) Engineer
Core tasks: Install and maintain heating, air conditioning and ventilation systems. Duties include erecting ductwork, piping refrigeration/AC lines, installing boilers and chillers, and programming controls. Electricians and plumbers often cross-train in HVAC. NCS lists tasks like following blueprints, installing equipment, fault-finding and maintenance.
Daily activities: Survey sites, cut and join ducts/pipes, install air handlers and heat exchangers, set up controls, test airflow and temperatures. May fit heat pumps, mechanical ventilation systems. Often work on building services contracts or major refurbishments.
Training: Typically an apprenticeship in heating/ventilation or Building Services Engineering (Level 3). Related qualifications: City & Guilds in refrigeration/Air Conditioning, Gas Safety. BTEC Engineering diplomas available. Modern skills include knowledge of HVAC regulations and energy efficiency. Mandatory: CSCS card. Short courses in refrigeration (F-Gas), BPEC certificates.
Skills/requirements: Mechanical/electrical aptitude. Ability to interpret technical drawings. Working at height and in confined spaces requires good balance. Physical strength (heavy equipment). Good communication (often team-based work on large systems). Some roles require CIBSE or chartership on career progression.
Tools: Sheet metal tools (crimpers, shears) for ducts, pipe tools (presses, flanges), drills, multimeters, refrigerant gauges. Ladders/scaffold for high ceiling/roof work. PPE: ear protection (noisy plant), dust masks, harnesses when climbing.
Salary: ~GBP 20k-GBP 40k. Specialist refrigeration or high-level design roles pay more. Overtime on call-out contracts adds. Region: London/SE trades up earnings.
Progression: Become Senior HVAC Engineer, Building Services Manager or project manager. Can specialise (e.g. Green Technologies, BIM for HVAC). Management paths: M&E (Mechanical & Electrical) Coordinator or Chartered Engineer (CEng) with further study.
Outlook: Demand from new-build and retrofits for energy-efficient HVAC (heat recovery, smart HVAC controls). Skills shortage notable in F-Gas certified engineers. Transition to low-carbon tech (heat pumps, ventilation systems) is expanding roles.
Employers/Setting: M&E contractors, facilities management companies, heating and AC service firms. Sites include commercial buildings, factories, hospitals. Often indoor mechanical rooms or outdoor AC units. Travel to multiple sites common.
H&S risks: Heavy lifting, electrical hazards in electrical components, working above ceilings or outdoors on roofs. Chemical exposure to refrigerants. PPE: hard hats, gloves, eyewear, hearing protection. Gas/pressure safety training critical.
Hours/Conditions: ~37-45h, with shift patterns for 24/7 maintenance contracts. Often daytime, but emergency call-outs at any time. Union: often GMB/Unite. Chartered institutions: CIBSE for senior engineers.
Entry Tips: Pursue an Advanced HVAC apprenticeship. Gain refrigerant handling certification (F-Gas). Start with basic plumbing or electrical knowledge. Relevant short courses (e.g. NEC3 contracts, PASMA) and an understanding of regulations (BSRIA guides, CIBSE publications) boost employability.
Roofer / Roofing Operative
Core tasks: Cover roofs with waterproof materials (tiles, slates, membranes) or thatching. Remove old roof coverings, fit insulation and underlayment, install roof vents and flashings. Also repair damaged roofs and work with chimneys. The NCS profile notes tasks like removing tiles, measuring and fitting waterproof sheets, and building structures using beams and boards
.
Daily activities: Climb scaffolds/ladders to access roofs; strip old tile or felt; nail or screw new shingles, tile or thatch; align and seal ridge lines; install gutters. Ensure edges are weather-tight. Work in pairs or teams for large roofs.
Training: Apprenticeship (2-3 years). NVQ in Roofing Occupations (specialisms like slate, tile or thatch). Many roofers start as general labourers (gaining CSCS card) then specialise. Qualifications in working at height, PASMA for mobile scaffolds, and CITB test for CSCS card. Thatchers need specific NVQs and may join Thatching Associations.
Skills/requirements: Good balance and no fear of heights; physical strength (lifting battens, slates). Work-safe mindset (slippery/steep surfaces). Teamwork (lifting heavy materials together). Basic knowledge of weatherproofing and roof design.
Tools: Ladders, roof harnesses, slate cutters, roofing nails, hammers or nail guns, waterproof gloves, knee pads (for tile roofing). Scaffolding equipment. PPE: helmets, fall-arrest harness, non-slip boots.
Salary: ~GBP 26k-GBP 42k
. Piecework or overtime common on large projects, boosting wages. London location premium. Thatchers can earn significantly more due to specialist skills.
Progression: Senior Roofer -> Site Supervisor -> Roofing Contractor (self-employed). Specialise as thatcher or asbestos roof remover (requires licensing). Transition to building surveying or joinery in roofing niches.
Outlook: Solid demand from roof refurbishments and new construction. Energy concerns increase demand for insulated roofs and solar roof integration (leading roofers to partner with solar installers
). Skilled roofing labor is scarce in UK (HSE/CITB note slow retirement of old workers).
Employers/Setting: Roofing contractors, construction firms, local councils (social housing). Mainly outdoor (roofs of houses, commercial buildings) in all weathers. Sometimes interior attic access. Usually manual, dusty, noisy.
H&S risks: Falls from height, dropped tools, sun exposure, manual handling. PPE: full-body harness with lifelines, safety nets, gloves. Frequent site toolbox talks on fall prevention.
Hours/Conditions: 40-44h/week
, Mon-Fri typical; overtime for weather delays. Early starts to use daylight. Work at heights in all weather seasons. Union: Roofing Contractors Association (trade group) and Unite/GMB representation.
Entry Tips: Complete CITB health & safety test and get CSCS card. Join a roofing apprenticeship (tile or slate specialism). Learn basic carpentry first to understand roof structures. Safety courses (e.g. IPAF for mobile platforms) help.
Painter & Decorator
Core tasks: Apply paint, varnish and wallpaper to interior/exterior surfaces. Duties include preparing surfaces (sanding, filling holes), mixing/pouring paints, applying coatings with brushes/rollers/sprayers, hanging wallpaper, and other finishes. The NCS role outlines tasks like "paint walls and doors, apply external finishes, spray apply paints"
.
Daily activities: Travel between sites (homes, offices), prep rooms (move furniture, dust), tape edges, apply primer and paint layers, paint ceilings/floors/exteriors, mix decorative finishes, clean brushes. Ensure smooth, even coats. Occasionally apply protective coatings (anti-mould, woodstain).
Training: Painting & decorating apprenticeship (Level 2-3) or college diploma. Key skills: colour theory, surface chemistry. NVQs/CSS certificates available. A CSCS card and CITB induction test mandatory. Optional city & guilds in industrial painting (for specialist), PAT test knowledge if involved in decorating with electrical equipment.
Skills/requirements: Attention to detail, steady hand and good vision (to detect flaws). Good colour perception and artistic sense. Able to work from scaffolds/ladders. Patience and physical stamina (kneeling, reaching). Basic drawing/measuring for wallpaper layout.
Tools: Brushes, rollers, paint sprayer, drop cloths, masks (for spray), sanders, putty knives, wallpapering tools (rollers, pasting table), extension poles. Safety gear: respirators (for fumes), coveralls, goggles, gloves.
Salary: ~GBP 17k-GBP 32k
. Higher rates for industrial/bridge painting (protective coatings). Overtime (evenings/weekends for minimal disturbance) adds pay. Contractors may charge GBP 100-GBP 150/day. Geographic variation modest (demand nationwide).
Progression: Lead painter/foreman -> Contracts Manager for painting trade. Some become interior designers or specialist restorers. Self-employment is common. Opportunities in conservation (heritage painting) with extra heritage skills.
Outlook: Steady demand from residential refurbishment and new builds. Energy-efficiency (cool roofs, anti-condensation coatings) create new product skills. Demand stable but competitive; skilled tradespeople leaving industry has some impact.
Employers/Setting: Painting contractors, building companies, facilities management firms. Work settings vary: domestic homes, commercial offices, schools, factories or outdoors (bridges, buildings). Many works indoors; exterior painting possible year-round (with weather constraints).
H&S risks: Fumes/inhalation (use ventilators, masks), skin contact with chemicals (gloves). Fall hazard from ladders. Noise from spray guns. PPE: masks/respirators, goggles, coveralls. Solvent fire risk in enclosed spaces.
Hours/Conditions: 40-47h/week
, often Monday-Friday, though evenings/weekends common to avoid disturbing occupants
. Work on bank holidays if needed (e.g. project deadline). Typically unionised via T&G (GMB now) for rates.
Entry Tips: Start with a Level 2 painting apprenticeship or NVQ. Short courses: health & safety (IOSH or CITB), specialist (e.g. industrial painting, spray certification). Practice DIY decorating at home to build basic skills.
Plasterer / Renderer
Core tasks: Apply plaster or rendering to walls/ceilings. Work includes mixing plaster, applying coats (skim, float finish), embedding reinforcement, smoothing surfaces and applying decorative finishes (stucco, mouldings)
. Often precedes painting/decorating.
Daily activities: Prepare surfaces (wetting walls, fixing lath or meshes), apply base coats, trowel on plaster to precise thickness, use floats for smoothness, carve details or apply textured finishes. Interior (dry lining) and exterior (cement render) tasks.
Training: Apprenticeship (Level 2-3). NVQs in plastering and drylining. GCSEs helpful for geometry. NVQ or technical diploma includes weatherproofing. CSCS card and CITB test required. Optional courses: SEMA (suspended ceilings) and plaster mouldings.
Skills/requirements: Strong arms and good hand-eye coordination for trowelling. Attention to detail (smooth finish). Good balance. Stamina - kneeling and overhead work. Basic maths for levels.
Tools: Trowels, hawk boards, floats, mix drills, straight edges, corner keys. Harness/scaffold for high ceilings. PPE: goggles (finesse work), gloves (from cement), dust mask when sanding.
Salary: ~GBP 21k-GBP 38k
. Top-end (decorative plasterers, setting plasterers) earn more. Overtime common to meet project schedules.
Progression: Foreman or Site Supervisor in drylining/plastering. Specialist plasterwork (cornices, ornamental) often commands higher pay. Branch into training others or surveying defects.
Outlook: Consistent demand in new construction and refurbishments. Skills shortage in UK as many plasterers retire. Drylining (for thermal/fire performance) is an expanding area.
Employers/Setting: Plastering companies, construction firms, residential developers. Site conditions vary: indoor (weatherproof) or scaffold/external work (cold).
H&S risks: Cement dust (respiratory hazard), falls (scaffold), physical strain. PPE: respiratory mask, goggles, gloves. Mixing plaster requires care (biological risk from airborne spores).
Hours/Conditions: ~40h/week, Monday-Friday, sometimes longer in warm weather (plaster dries quicker). Often unionised (Unite/GMB). Entry: take introductory plastering courses, seek assistant roles to gain experience.
Tiler (Floor & Wall)
Core tasks: Lay ceramic, stone, or porcelain tiles on floors and walls. Tasks include measuring layouts, cutting tiles, applying adhesive/grout, and finishing joints
. Specialties include bathroom/kitchen tiling or exterior paving.
Daily activities: Calculate tile quantities, prepare and level surfaces, mix adhesives, cut tiles to fit with tile saws, lay patterns (e.g. brick bond), apply grout, seal tiles, clean and polish finished floors/walls. Work on new builds or refits.
Training: Tiling apprenticeship (Level 2). NVQs in Wall & Floor Tiling. Tilers often start as general labourers. Some take City & Guilds Tile courses. CSCS card required. Short courses in wetroom installation.
Skills/requirements: Manual precision and good eye for patterns. Patience for repetitive work. Standing/crouching on hard floors requires stamina and knee protection. Good maths (angles, areas) for cutting layouts.
Tools: Tile cutters (manual, wet saw), spatulas, trowels for adhesive, laser levels, grout floats, sponges. PPE: kneepads, safety glasses (sawing tile), dust mask.
Salary: ~GBP 21k-GBP 38k
. Premium for specialist tiling (e.g. porcelain, mosaic). Overtime on large projects increases earnings.
Progression: Lead Tiler -> Contracts Manager/Estimator. Could branch into stone masonry or floor layers (wood). Tiling instructors or business owners often tile part-time.
Outlook: Demand from high-spec housing, bathrooms/kitchens refits and tiling in public buildings. Skill shortage is moderate (skilled tilers valued).
Employers/Setting: Tiling contractors, construction companies, specialist tilers. Sites include homes, commercial interiors (offices, shops), sometimes outdoor paving. Mostly indoor work.
H&S risks: Slips (wet tiles/adhesive), cuts from tile edges/saws. Proper ventilation when cutting hard materials. PPE: goggles, masks, knee pads.
Hours/Conditions: ~40-45h/week. Standard day hours, though planning may require early start. Often project-based contract (some self-employed).
Glazier
Core tasks: Measure, cut and fit glass into windows, doors, shop-fronts, conservatories and partitions. This includes selecting appropriate glass (toughened, double-glazed), installing frames/seals and ensuring weather-tightness. The NCS profile notes tasks like using suction lifters to position glass and sealing edges
.
Daily activities: Visit site to take precise measurements, cut glass to size, assemble glazing units, lift and fix panes into frames (aluminium, wood, PVC), apply sealant, and install protective mesh if needed (security glazing). May repair broken windows or install mirrors.
Training: Glazing apprenticeship (Level 2). NVQ in Glass and Glazing. Experience handling large glass sheets is key; courses in facade work or IGU (insulated glass units) help. CSCS card, manual handling and height safety courses required.
Skills/requirements: Excellent coordination and spatial sense. Ability to work carefully with heavy, fragile materials. Stamina for carrying glass. Good eyesight and focus (grain-free cuts). Knowledge of glass types and thicknesses.
Tools: Glass suction lifter/clamps, glass cutter, grinders/polishers, measuring tapes, silicone guns. Ladders and scaffolding for high installations. PPE: cut-resistant gloves, safety glasses, hard hats (glass is heavy).
Salary: ~GBP 23k-GBP 40k
. Specialty (curved or high-performance glazing) commands more. Installation of large building facades yields higher pay.
Progression: Lead glazier or shop foreman. Facade or shopfitting project manager. Can pivot to architectural glass design or glass manufacturing. Some move into window cleaning/maintenance business.
Outlook: Stable demand in building construction/renovation. Modern architecture's glass use (double/triple glazing, insulated facades) increases workload. Shortage of skilled commercial glaziers is noted in industry (glass training is specialized).
Employers/Setting: Glazing firms, joinery or building contractors, glazing shops. Work on domestic (homes, schools) and commercial (offices, retail, skyscrapers). Often on scaffolds or lifts for large panels. Both indoor/outdoor.
H&S risks: Glass shards (wear thick gloves), falls from heights, heavy lifting injuries. PPE: cut-resistant gear, harnesses on ladders, face shields. Secure glass to avoid dropping.
Hours/Conditions: ~40h/week; peaks in spring/summer (better weather). Unions: Unite/GMB cover glazing; European Glass Industry Training likely to help standards.
Scaffolder
Core tasks: Erect and dismantle temporary structures (scaffolding) that give safe access to buildings. Tasks include assembling steel/board frames, securing planks, fitting guardrails and safety nets
. Scaffolders follow building progress, climb to new heights and ensure all access is safe and legal.
Daily activities: Unload scaffold materials, read scaffold plans, assemble sections (standards, ledgers, transoms), bolt/check fittings, attach toe boards and nets. Inspect for structural integrity daily. Dismantle scaffolding as project ends. Frequently climb to adjust working platforms.
Training: 3-4 year scaffolding apprenticeship (Level 2/3). NVQ in Accessing the Built Environment. Must attend IPAF (powered access), PASMA (mobile towers) training, and CITB HS&E test. CSCS Scaffolder card mandatory. Short courses in advanced rigging or tube-fitting.
Skills/requirements: Great physical fitness and balance; strong upper body for lifting parts. No fear of heights. Attention to detail for safety checks. Team communication skills (often work in crews).
Tools: Wrenches, hammers, clips, ladders, harnesses, board lifters. PPE: hard hats, gloves, steel-toe boots, high-vis.
Salary: ~GBP 25k-GBP 51k. Premium for complex or high-level work (tower cranes, multi-storey). Overtime and night shifts (for minimal public disruption) pay extra. ONS data shows scaffolder wages above many trades.
Progression: Advanced Scaffolder -> Site Supervisor or Scaffold Inspector (requires CISRS certification) -> Training (CISRS Instructor). Some scaffolders become Site Managers or move into related fields (steel erection, rigging, or safety oversight).
Outlook: Essential for all construction. Shortage of qualified scaffolders in UK (CISRS issues 4,000+ cards/year). Demand tied to overall building activity. More stringent safety regs keep work steady.
Employers/Setting: Specialist scaffolding companies; some large construction firms hire own scaffold gangs. Work on all construction sites (roads, buildings). Outdoors in all weather; on rooftops/scaffolds. Must access high/odd places.
H&S risks: Falls from height, dropped tools/boards, strike hazards. Strict safety training: use harnesses, guardrails, toe boards. Daily inspections critical. PPE mandatory (helmets, non-slip boots).
Hours/Conditions: ~40-48h/week
. Might work weekends or nights to finish scaffolding before tradespeople move in. Usually unionised (UCATT/Unite historically active).
Plant & Crane Operators
Core tasks: Operate heavy machinery (excavators, bulldozers, loaders) or mobile cranes to move earth, lift materials and build infrastructure. For cranes: lift, swing, and place steel, concrete panels and containers. For plant: dig foundations, haul aggregates, grade land. The NCS "Construction Plant Operator" role lists tasks like hauling, digging, bridging gaps
, and [102+L168-L174] mentioned crane operations.
Daily activities: Inspect machine, maneuver equipment per site plan, load/unload materials, backfill trenches, clear debris. Crane operators set up base and outriggers, rig loads (with a slinger banksman), and precisely place structural components. Both roles coordinate with site teams via radio/signals.
Training: Heavy plant apprenticeships (Level 2) or on-the-job training; CPCS or NPORS certification for specific machines. Crane operators need a CSCS card and CPCS Crane Operator ticket (Tower or Mobile cranes). Regular refresher training (CPCS renewals every 5 years). Class 1 or 2 driving license often required.
Skills/requirements: Mechanical aptitude; steady nerves (moving heavy loads). Good spatial awareness for operating in tight sites. Ability to judge weight and distance. Required health: excellent eyesight and hearing. Usually must pass medical for crane licenses. Strong concentration and communication (to coordinate lifts).
Equipment: Excavators, bulldozers, cranes, telehandlers, dump trucks, etc. Tools: maintenance tools (grease gun, torque wrenches). For cranes: block, wire rope, hooks, spreader beams. PPE: High-vis, helmet, hearing protection (machines are loud).
Salary: ~GBP 25k-GBP 45k
. Crane operators often earn more (specialised skill). Overtime (weekend/night shifts) common. London/construction hotspots pay 10-15% higher.
Progression: Senior/Lead Operator, Site Plant Coordinator. Master Lorry Crane license (for tower/overhead). Site management (Plant Manager) roles or training/instructor positions (CPCS examiner). Some move into mechanical maintenance.
Outlook: Strong demand with growth in infrastructure/renewables. Shortage of trained crane operators in UK; aging workforce. Automated tech (GPS-controlled diggers, teleoperation) emerging but still limited.
Employers/Setting: Civil engineering contractors, building firms, utilities (for excavations), shipping yards (cranes). Work on construction sites (building, roads, bridges), quarries, ports. Mostly outdoors.
H&S risks: Machine rollovers, tip-overs, struck-by hazards. Crane drop hazards. Must follow LOLER (lifting regs), PPE (seat belts, guards). Daily walk-round checks and maintenance are mandatory.
Hours/Conditions: 40-45h+ (48h/week cap under Working Time Directive). Shifts to cover site schedules; possible nights (roadworks) or weekends. Union: GMB often represents operatives. Professional standards by CITB and funding for simulator training.
Site Manager / Construction Manager
Core duties: Plan and oversee building projects on-site. Manage labour, subcontractors and schedules to ensure work meets plans, quality standards and safety regulations. Duties include coordinating trades, ordering materials, supervising progress and liaising with clients/engineers.
Typical activities: Conduct daily briefings, inspect work quality, ensure health & safety compliance, resolve delays, update construction programmes. Handle documentation (permitting, reports) and client meetings. May also troubleshoot technical issues or design changes.
Qualifications: Usually a degree in Construction Management or Building Services, or professional experience plus relevant certifications (e.g. CIOB or chartership). Many start as qualified tradespeople or graduates. Apprenticeship routes (Higher/Degree) exist (CITB Foundations, or degree apprenticeship). CSCS Gold Card often held by managers. Chartered Institution membership (CIOB) is common for career progression.
Skills: Strong leadership, communication, planning and budgeting skills. Knowledge of construction methods and British Standards. Problem-solving under pressure. The role requires being on feet at site and visiting risky areas (so meet basic fitness and health requirements).
Tools: Computers/tablet with BIM/CAD software, scheduling tools (Primavera/MS Project), digital cameras, surveying equipment (for layout). On-site: pens, folders, measuring tapes. PPE (helmet/boots) when touring sites.
Salary: ~GBP 27k-GBP 65k (wide range by experience and project scale). Median ~GBP 45k. London/mega-projects pay top-end (often GBP 60k+). Company directors and large firms pay more, including bonuses.
Progression: Project Manager -> Programme Director or Senior Manager. Many achieve professional chartership (MCIOB/FCIOB). Some become specialist consultants, or start their own contracting firms.
Outlook: High demand as construction volume grows. Site managers are critical for quality and safety (in shortage according to CITB). Modern drivers include Offsite construction, digital tech (BIM) and sustainability standards (BREEAM).
Employers/Setting: Building contractors, civil engineering firms, public agencies. Work is both in site offices and field. Projects include schools, hospitals, infrastructure, residential estates. Often multi-site management roles in large firms.
H&S risks: Even as managers, they must enforce safety; occasionally entering unsafe zones (so PPE). Main risk is stress; ensure health/safety training. Leadership of H&S team is key (often has deputy H&S officer).
Hours/Conditions: ~50-60h/week common (deadlines/shift handovers). Typically weekdays, with occasional weekend work. High responsibility role - usually unionised only at junior levels. Professional bodies: CIOB, ICE (for civil engineers who manage), BRE (sustainability).
Entry Tips: Civil or construction degree or higher apprenticeship (e.g. CITB's degree apprenticeship). Gain experience via a trade or site supervisor role. Develop computer skills (BIM) and take health & safety (NEBOSH) and management courses.
Quantity Surveyor (QS)
Core duties: Manage all financial aspects of construction projects. This includes cost estimating, tendering, contract administration, and final accounting. A QS tallies bills of quantities, negotiates contracts with suppliers/subcontractors, and ensures projects stay within budget. Tasks from NCS: determine needs, cost materials, negotiate contracts, monitor costs and advise on legal issues.
Daily activities: Prepare cost plans from drawings, issue tender documents, evaluate bids, track expenditures, forecast overruns, compile interim and final payment claims. Advise architects/clients on value engineering and commercial decisions throughout construction.
Qualifications: Often a degree in quantity surveying or construction management (e.g. BSc QS). Many are Chartered Surveyors (RICS) or SCSI. Some enter via HND/degree apprenticeships. Technical certifications in software (CostX, Cubit) and contracts (NEC/SFIDIC) are highly valued.
Skills: Strong numeracy and attention to detail. Good communication (liaise with all stakeholders). Negotiation and knowledge of UK contract law (JCT, FIDIC). Analytical mindset to assess project changes.
Tools: Cost-estimating software (e.g. Bluebeam, Excel). BIM can generate material quantities. Standard office tools (Word, email). Calculators and databases of standard rates. Professional books (BCIS).
Salary: ~GBP 26k-GBP 70k (senior QS on big projects earns top-end). London QS salaries exceed regional ones (often 10-20% higher). Bonuses and profit share common in consultancy.
Progression: Senior Quantity Surveyor -> Commercial Manager -> Director. Alternative paths: become a chartered accountant focusing on construction, or move to roles in procurement/planning. Some switch to cost consultant or develop property.
Outlook: High demand in infrastructure and large-scale builds. QS skills in shortage (CIOB and RICS note insufficient grads). New drivers: offsite prefabrication (QS integrates offsite costs) and sustainability (green materials).
Employers/Setting: Employers include construction firms, QS/consultancy practices, property developers and government agencies. Work is mostly in offices, but frequent site visits to verify works. Usually surrounded by desk- and meetings-work.
H&S risks: Mostly office-based, but site checks require PPE (hard hats, boots). Site hazards are minimal risk as QS. Maintain up-to-date CDM awareness (they advise on contractual risk allocations).
Hours/Conditions: 37-40h/week, Mon-Fri; some overtime during bid periods. Often salary-based; professional industry association: RICS or Institute of Surveyors. Entry: degrees (RICS accredited), APC chartership. Short courses: ICD or CIOB certificates, and project management (APM/PMP) help.
Civil Engineer (Construction)
Core duties: Design and oversee construction of infrastructure (roads, bridges, tunnels, drainage). Civil engineers draft plans, compute structural integrity, manage projects and ensure compliance with regulations. Tasks (from NCS): design foundations, supervise construction, and collaborate with clients and architects
.
Typical tasks: Perform site surveys and feasibility studies; produce structural calculations; supervise earthworks; coordinate with sub-disciplines (geotech, water engineering); ensure works meet spec; conduct inspections/test outcomes; often use modelling software.
Qualifications: Degree in civil engineering (BEng/MEng). Chartered status (CEng/MICE) is common. Graduate schemes or NVQ Level 6 (Higher Apprenticeship) exist. Professional licensure (Engineering Council UK) required for senior roles. Masters/PhDs for specializations (e.g. environmental).
Skills: Strong analytical and maths skills. Proficiency with CAD/BIM software and hydraulics/geotech knowledge. Project management, teamwork, and communication with stakeholders. Physically, must inspect sites (no major health restrictions beyond general fitness).
Tools: Civil design software (AutoCAD Civil 3D, MicroStation), structural analysis programs (STAAD, Midas), surveying instruments (total station, GPS), engineering calculators.
Salary: ~GBP 29k-GBP 63k
. Chartered engineers in London on mega-projects can exceed GBP 70k. Government/pylons projects (Severn Bridge, HS2) pay premiums.
Progression: Senior Engineer -> Project/Programme Manager -> Director. Consultants may open firms. With experience, can move into specialist roles (hydraulic/structural/site investigation). Also move to academia or lecturing.
Outlook: Very strong demand (infrastructure schemes, flood defence, transportation). UK Skills shortages in civil engineering; ageing workforce. Growth areas: climate adaptation (flood defences), renewable structures, smart cities.
Employers/Setting: Civil contractors (BAM, Laing O'Rourke), consultancies (AECOM, Arup), local authorities, utilities. Work is split between site offices and field (survey, inspection) on projects from highways to dams.
H&S risks: Site visits entail typical construction hazards (falls, machinery), but engineer roles are low-risk. Always wear PPE when on active sites. Usually sit at desk for analysis, so ergonomic issues.
Hours/Conditions: 37-40h standard; project deadlines may cause longer days. Client meetings or site emergencies can require occasional travel/odd hours. Professional body: Institution of Civil Engineers (ICE). Entry: accredited degree; short courses: PRINCE2, AutoCAD, BIM.
Surveyor (Land/Building)
Core tasks: Surveyors assess land and property. Land Surveyors measure land features and boundaries, produce maps and plans for construction
. Building Surveyors inspect structures for defects and compliance (not separately listed). Basic NCS data for "Land surveyor" lists tasks like measuring site boundaries, using surveying instruments, and advising on development permissions
.
Daily activities: Land: set out site markers, level ground, record topographic data, use GPS/total stations, produce plans. H&S risks: Field work involves normal site hazards (uneven terrain, traffic). PPE: boots, high-vis. For building surveys, minimal risk beyond clutter/uneven floors.
Hours/Conditions: ~37-40h/week. Seasonal fieldwork possible (land surveying). RICS and CICES (Chartered Institution of Civil Engineering Surveyors) set standards. Entry: accredited degree, APC chartership.
Estimator (Construction)
Core tasks: Prepare detailed cost estimates for projects. Duties include reading drawings, calculating material and labour quantities, pricing items (materials, plant, labour), compiling tender bids and cost reports
. Ensures bids are competitive and profit targets are met.
Daily activities: Measure quantities from plans, source suppliers' prices, input into cost databases, liaise with subcontractors, produce bill of quantities, risk assess and include contingencies. Track tendering process and report outcomes.
Qualifications: Background in construction or commerce; many have degrees or HND in Quantity Surveying or Construction Estimating. Internal training is common. Often comes from a trade or QS background. Familiarity with building regs and procurement. No mandatory cert.
Skills: High numeracy and IT skills (Excel, costing software). Good knowledge of construction methods to avoid omissions. Time management (tender deadlines) and negotiation with suppliers.
Tools: Estimating packages (CostX, CCS), spreadsheets, take-off software, BIM cost add-ons. Industry pricing guides (BCIS, Spon's).
Salary: ~GBP 22k-GBP 40k
. Senior estimators on large projects can exceed GBP 50k. Commission or bonuses based on winning bids not uncommon. London positions pay more.
Progression: Chief Estimator -> Commercial Manager or Director of Estimating. Some move into QS or Contracts Management. Others become independent cost consultants.
Outlook: Skilled estimators are in demand as bids drive business. Emphasis on accuracy in modern tight-margin environment. Skills gap exists as experienced estimators retire. Tech (BIM cost estimation) is a growth area.
Employers/Setting: Typically employed by contractors (housebuilders, civil firms) or trade contractors. Primarily office-based, with site visits for complex projects. Work also in client-side (consultant estimating).
H&S risks: Office role - minimal. Site visits require standard PPE.
Hours/Conditions: ~37-40h/week; longer hours near bid deadlines. Mainly weekdays. Professional body: Association for Project Management (APM) or training like ACostE (Association of Cost Engineers). Entry: construction management or diploma; short courses in advanced estimating tools.
Health & Safety Officer (Construction)
Core duties: Ensure workplace safety and compliance with regulations. Tasks include writing risk assessments, conducting site audits, training staff in safe practices, investigating incidents and liaising with regulators. Duties listed include implementing safety systems, monitoring legislation and advising management
.
Daily activities: Perform site inspections, lead toolbox talks, review safety permits, maintain records (incident logs, training certificates), issue permits (hot works, confined space). Advise on accident prevention, and update method statements.
Qualifications: Degree or diploma in H&S (e.g. NEBOSH, NVQ Level 6). IOSH or graduate membership preferred. Chartership (CMIOSH) for senior roles. Often previous trade experience. Mandatory CITB HS&E course not enough; HSE/HSE officers require professional qualifications.
Skills: Extensive knowledge of UK H&S law (Health & Safety at Work, CDM, PUWER, etc.). Analytical for investigations. Communication skills to train and enforce rules. Stamina to inspect sites daily. Infield physical fitness needed for surveys.
Tools: Inspection checklists, audit software. PPE (when visiting sites, always full kit). IT: spreadsheets/database for accident stats.
Salary: ~GBP 26k-GBP 48k
. Senior H&S managers on large sites can exceed GBP 50k. Public sector often lower than private. Safety-critical industries pay premiums.
Progression: Senior H&S Manager, Regional Safety Director or HSE Consultant. With experience, can branch into training (NEBOSH trainer) or regulatory roles (HSE inspector).
Outlook: Vital role - demand grows with tightening regulations (e.g. new Building Safety Act). HSE and trade bodies report a shortage of qualified safety professionals. Site safety focus increases due to enforcement.
Employers/Setting: Every construction firm, developer or contractor. Employers range from housebuilding groups to infrastructure firms. Work split: office (paperwork, planning) and site (inspections). Regular attendance at active sites.
H&S risks: Ironically, H&S officers must occasionally enter dangerous zones; PPE is strict. Main risk is office-related (desk work). They enforce compliance, so must avoid complacency.
Hours/Conditions: ~40h/week, typically Mon-Fri. On-call for incident investigations. Usually unionised indirectly (BIFM - British Institute of Facilities Management - for FM H&S, or IOSH affiliation). Entry: NEBOSH Dip or equivalent, plus IOSH Managing Safely course recommended; learn incident investigation (TapRoot/CAP) and safety legislation updates.
Building Inspector / Control Officer
Core duties: Inspect construction work for compliance with Building Regulations and standards. A Building Control (BC) Officer reviews plans, inspects stages (foundations, structure, insulation, fire safety) and issues completion certificates. The NCS building control profile lists tasks like approving plans, inspecting construction stages and enforcing regulations
.
Daily activities: Examine drawings for regulation compliance; carry out scheduled site visits; test/measure completed work (e.g. insulation U-values); report non-compliance (requiring corrections). Interact with builders to explain requirements. Issue official paperwork (notices, certificates).

Progression: Senior/Chief Building Surveyor or Manager, then to head of BCO department or technical consultancy. Some move into fire safety engineering or construction law advisory.
Outlook: Demand steady: government emphasizes building safety (post-Grenfell reforms). Need for inspectors rising. Digital building (BIM) means inspectors adapt to 3D models. Chronic shortage in building control officers is an industry issue.
Employers/Setting: Local government building control departments or private Approved Inspectors. Office-based prep of documentation with frequent travel to sites (inspection of new/renovated buildings).
H&S risks: Occasional site visits involve construction hazards; strict adherence to PPE. Often supervise compliance, not engage in manual work, so office H&S minimal.
Hours/Conditions: ~37-40h/week, weekdays. Some emergencies or extended visits may occur. Professional bodies: Local Authority Building Control (LABC) for public sector; Registration with Approved Inspectors Register (AIR) for private. Entry: degree in survey/pract. (RICS APC needed), Building Control diplomas, or experienced site managers retraining.
Demolition Operative
Core tasks: Demolish and dismantle structures safely. Duties include manual dismantling (gutting interiors, removing windows/doors), mechanical demolition with excavators, separating debris, and disposing of hazardous materials (like asbestos)
. Building on the NCS profile, tasks include setting up safety zones, stripping non-structural elements, operating cutting machinery, and controlling waste streams
.
Daily activities: Read demolition plans; erect barriers and dust-control measures; remove fittings (plumbing, wiring); cut metal beams with torches; operate excavators/crushers; load debris; sort recyclable materials; handle removal of dangerous materials per regulation.

. Hazard pay often included. Self-employed demolition contractors can earn significantly more. London higher end.
Progression: Advanced Demolition Operative (specialist cutting), Plant Operator (larger machines), Site Supervisor or Safety Officer for demolition. Some transition to civil earthworks.
Outlook: Steady/strong demand as old stock is cleared and sites prepared. MMR (mega mining) booms and urban renewal increase demolition needs. Strict safety culture means highly skilled workers are required.
Employers/Setting: Demolition contractors (often civil companies), utility clearance crews. Highly hazardous site environments. Mostly outdoors; some interior work. Many projects in city redevelopment.
H&S risks: High risk: structural collapse, falling debris, noise, dust (silica/asbestos). Strict adherence to HSE demolition regs is mandatory. PPE always full: helmets, harnesses on high structures, visors, dust masks, hearing protection. Explosive methods involve licensed specialists.
Hours/Conditions: ~40h/week
. Irregular scheduling (project-driven). Work occasionally nights (implosion) or weekends. Unions: UCATT/Unite historically; CSCS Demolition card required. Entry tips: CSCS card, attend demolition safety training (some HSE-funded courses).

Daily activities: Conduct site feasibility, order specialized equipment, integrate with existing electrical systems (must involve electricians for mains connection). Possibly perform microinverter or tracker installations.
Training: Electrical background is essential. Common route: electrician apprenticeship plus short renewable energy courses (MCS certification for PV installers). CITB offers "Renewable energy technician" courses.
Skills/requirements: Combines electrical and roofing skills. Good at working at height and understanding of solar tech/electronics. CSCS, 18th Edition regs, MCS install cert.
Tools: Screwdrivers, wrenches, drills, crimping tools, ladders or cherry-pickers. Test equipment (PV optimisers, multimeters). PPE: harness, fall arrest, gloves.
Salary: ~GBP 25k-GBP 47k
. Government incentives have raised pay; skilled installers in remote or domestic settings charge more.
Progression: Master Electrician -> Renewable project engineer. Could move into green energy consulting or management.
Outlook: Strong growth (net zero targets). Skills shortage in installers is noted. Industry support via Solar Trades Association.
Employers/Setting: Renewable installers, electrical contractors, roofing firms. Roof-top or ground-mount. Outdoor work (sun, heat).
H&S risks: Falls, electrical work hazards. PPE: harnesses, insulated tools. Awareness of asbestos if older roofs.
Hours/Conditions: Typical construction hours, with seasonal slowdown (winter).
Smart Home Technology Installer
Core tasks: Install integrated home systems (lighting, security, audio, HVAC controls). Duties include fitting controllers, connecting smart devices (locks, sensors, panels) and configuring networks
.
Daily activities: Design system layouts with clients, run wiring/cables (often low-voltage), install hubs and smart appliances, program system via computer, demonstrate usage. Tasks as per NCS: install, connect and calibrate controls; test circuits; provide user training
.
Training: Apprenticeship (Level 3) in smart home or network installation. Skilled electricians or AV technicians can transition. Certifications from CEDIA (Electronic Systems) or IT/network (CompTIA).
Skills/requirements: Tech-savvy (networks, computers, IoT). Good electrical knowledge (often licensed electrician). Detail-oriented for neat cable runs and system integration. Customer service oriented (education required).
Tools: Network tools (crimpers, testers), low-voltage cable pullers, drills. Laptops with programming software. PPE: minimal (standard for site work).
Salary: ~GBP 22k-GBP 55k
. Top-end for complex audiovisual/automation projects. Growing demand means potentially high earnings.
Progression: Senior installer/Project Manager or Home Automation Consultant. Specialisms (lighting design, AV cinematics). Many form small businesses.
Outlook: Rapid growth (smart tech adoption). High demand for integrators. Few trained installers; industry group CEDIA expects growth.
Employers/Setting: Specialist AV companies, electricians, new-build home builders. Works in homes, hotels, commercial offices. Indoor work, sometimes network back-ends.
H&S risks: Low-voltage nature - low electrical hazard. Working often at height for speaker/wire runs - harnesses needed. Standard site PPE.
Hours/Conditions: Standard construction hours; occasional weekend installations. Works are often interior and climate-controlled.
BIM Technician / CAD Technician - Unspecified

Duties: creating and managing digital 3D construction models, clash detection, coordinating multidisciplinary data. Skills: software expertise (Revit, Navisworks), construction knowledge. Salary ~GBP 25k-GBP 40k depending on experience. Employed by architects or large contractors; increasing demand due to BIM mandates.

Facilities Maintenance / Management (Building Services Engineer)
Core duties: Ensure building systems (electrical, HVAC, plumbing, fire safety) function smoothly. For a Facilities Manager, tasks include maintenance scheduling, contractor management and overseeing repairs. The NCS Facilities Manager profile outlines tasks like coordinating office moves, planning sustainability and managing budgets
. Building Services Engineer roles focus on system design and upkeep.
Daily activities: Manage helpdesk/repair requests, plan preventative maintenance, negotiate service contracts, oversee energy usage (often for efficiency goals), ensure compliance (gas safety, fire alarms), and handle space planning. NCS tasks: ensure services run smoothly, coordinate refurbishments, maintain H&S standards
.
Qualifications: Facilities Manager: degree or apprenticeship (Level 4-6 FM). Certifications (IWFM membership). Building Services Engineer: degree in Building Services, mechanical/electrical engineering. Chartered membership (CIBSE, IMechE). Trade background beneficial (electrical or plumbing skills for hands-on roles).
Skills: Management, budgeting and procurement. Technical knowledge of building systems. Leadership and communication.
Tools: FM software (maintenance management), building automation systems, energy meters. Technical: multimeter, ventilation testers. Office applications for planning.
Salary: ~GBP 26k-GBP 50k
. Senior FM or estate roles can exceed GBP 60k, especially in finance/large organizations.
Progression: Senior Facilities Director, Head of Estates (e.g. NHS Estates). Could branch to property development or corporate real estate roles.
Outlook: Growing with expanding commercial real estate sector. Emphasis on energy efficiency and smart buildings drives up skill requirements. Steady need for qualified FM professionals.
Employers/Setting: Corporate employers (hospitals, universities, retail chains) or FM firms. Typically based in offices (plant rooms) with site visits. Often 24/7 coverage (hospitals, hotels).
H&S risks: Office and building plant room visits pose minor risk (machinery pinch points, chemical storage). On-site maintenance work may involve typical trade hazards. PPE used when on plant.
Hours/Conditions: 38-42h/week
. Shift patterns likely (some roles require on-call for emergencies). Evening/weekend work to minimise disruption (system shut-downs). Professional association: IWFM (formerly BIFM).

Recommendations
Prioritise NVQ/Apprenticeships: For hands-on trades (bricklaying, plumbing, electrical, etc.) the established path is a Level 2-3 apprenticeship. Ensure any course or apprenticeship is CITB-accredited and includes the CSCS Health & Safety test.
Gain Core Certifications: Essential credentials include CSCS card (via CITB HS&E test), trade-specific licenses (e.g. Gas Safe for plumbers, CPCS for plant operators, ACS for gas engineers). Short courses (first aid, manual handling, working at height) are universally valuable.
Leverage Industry Bodies: Use Go Construct (CITB platform) and trade associations to find up-to-date training. Engage with relevant bodies (IET for electricians, CIBSE for HVAC, RICS for surveyors) early for networking and guidance.
Flexible Learning: For career changers (age 25+), consider adult apprenticeships or conversion courses (e.g. plumbing Bootcamps, Construction Skills certificates). Pair formal training with on-site work for experience.
Stay Informed of Regulations: Continuously update on UK Building Standards, BS 7671 (electrical), Gas Regs, CDM Regulations and energy efficiency standards, as compliance is a key part of most roles.
Health & Safety First: Always account for personal fitness and medical requirements (vision, hearing, cardiology for high-risk jobs). Employers and trainees should prioritise accredited safety training (IOSH/NEBOSH) and enforce PPE for all site roles.
Each role above may have regional salary variations (London/South-East higher; Northern and Scotland generally lower). This summary consolidates UK government and industry sources (NCS, CITB, HSE) to provide a comprehensive view of construction/trade careers
. The landscape of construction careers continues to evolve with technology and legislation, so ongoing training and flexibility are key to success.
