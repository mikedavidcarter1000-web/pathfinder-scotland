
Health and Medicine Careers Landscape
Executive summary
Health and medicine careers form a large ecosystem spanning regulated clinical professions, allied health and healthcare science, public health and prevention, research, operational leadership, informatics, and essential support services. Across major English-speaking health systems, many roles are "portable" in concept (e.g., nurse, radiographer, physiotherapist) but differ materially in training length, protected titles, and licensure processes by jurisdiction. Statutory registration is a central safety mechanism for many patient-facing roles (for example, UK protected titles under regulators overseen by the PSA, Ireland's multi-profession regulator CORU, and Australia's national registration scheme via AHPRA). 

Workforce demand remains structurally high in most systems due to ageing populations, long-term condition burden, and service backlogs. In England, the NHS Long Term Workforce Plan explicitly models substantial workforce growth needs (and training pipeline expansion) over the coming decade(s). 
 In the US, the BLS projects healthcare occupations overall to grow "much faster than average" from 2024-2034, with about 1.9 million openings per year on average across healthcare occupations (growth plus replacement needs). 

Pay is best treated as framework-based rather than a single number:

In the UK, many (not all) non-medical roles are paid on Agenda for Change (AfC) pay bands; the NHS Employers 2026/27 published band entry and top points provide a robust reference for NHS England-aligned roles. 
In Ireland, public service healthcare grades are published as consolidated HSE pay scales by grade (incremental scales). 
In the US, the BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook provides median pay and education levels for many health occupations (May 2024 medians on the healthcare occupation tables and individual occupation pages). 
In Australia, Jobs and Skills Australia occupation profiles provide median weekly earnings and occupation descriptors, plus registration notes (where applicable). 
In Canada, licensing is provincial/territorial, and national bodies support entry-to-practice examinations in several professions (e.g., NCLEX-RN for RNs; PEBC certification for pharmacy). 
Because no single official source enumerates every healthcare job title globally (and titles vary greatly by employer and country), this report uses an occupational-taxonomy approach: the list below is intended to be comprehensive across the main regulated professions and the most common job families in clinical care, allied health, public health, research, administration, informatics, support services, and emerging roles--while explicitly noting where role titles and requirements vary widely. (For context on breadth, NHS Health Careers publishes an A-Z careers list with many distinct roles). 

Scope, definitions, and how to read this report
This report focuses on licensing/registration pathways and role archetypes across five major English-speaking jurisdictions: United Kingdom (UK), Ireland (ROI), United States (US), Canada (canada), and Australia (australia). It is current to 14/04/2026 (Europe/London).

Key definitions used consistently:

Career: an occupational family with a recognisable scope of practice (e.g., "Registered Nurse", "Radiographer", "Clinical Scientist"), not every micro-specialty job title used by individual employers.
Licensing/registration: statutory regulation (protected title and/or legal requirement to register) where applicable; otherwise voluntary certification or employer credentialing.
Required qualifications and durations: typical entry pathways with common duration ranges. Where requirements vary widely (especially for counselling/psychotherapy and some informatics roles), that variability is stated explicitly.
Salary handling (important):

Many jurisdictions publish pay scales (range by grade/band and increments) rather than median wage; where median pay is unavailable or not comparable, the report marks pay as unspecified / varies and points to the best official framework for that jurisdiction.

Statutory regulation and key bodies
To avoid repeating long regulator names in every profile, abbreviations below are used consistently after first mention.

UK (statutory professional regulators)
The Professional Standards Authority for Health and Social Care oversees multiple UK health and care professional regulators. 
 Key regulators relevant to roles in this report include the General Medical Council (doctors; also regulates physician associates and anaesthesia associates from 12/2024), Nursing and Midwifery Council (nurses, midwives; nursing associates in England), Health and Care Professions Council (15 professions with protected titles), General Dental Council, General Pharmaceutical Council (GB pharmacists and pharmacy technicians), General Optical Council, General Osteopathic Council, General Chiropractic Council, and Social Work England. 

Ireland (statutory/profession regulators)
Medical Council registration is legally required for doctors to practise (specialist practice requires specialist registration). 
 Nursing and Midwifery Board of Ireland maintains the register for nurses and midwives. 
 Pharmaceutical Society of Ireland registers pharmacists and pharmaceutical assistants. 
 Dental Council of Ireland maintains dental registers (including hygienists and dental nurses; note dental nurse registration is stated as voluntary on the Council's auxiliary dental worker guidance). 
 CORU is the statutory multi-profession regulator for a defined set of health and social care professions; its list includes physiotherapists, radiographers/radiation therapists, medical scientists, dietitians, occupational therapists, podiatrists, speech and language therapists, psychologists, social workers, social care workers, and (in its legislation list) counsellors and psychotherapists, among others. 

US (state licensure model; national examinations)
Medicine is a licensed profession regulated by individual states; state medical boards issue physician licences, with pathways typically involving national examinations (commonly the USMLE sequence) plus postgraduate training. 
 Nursing licensure also operates via state boards, with the NCLEX administered through the National Council of State Boards of Nursing framework. 

Canada (provincial/territorial regulation; some national exams/certification)
Canada's health professional regulation sits with provincial/territorial regulators; governments have pursued mobility initiatives for "good standing" practitioners across jurisdictions. 
 For RNs, the Canadian Council of Registered Nurse Regulators notes that graduates applying to practise nursing must complete an entry-to-practice examination for the registration sought, and identifies the NCLEX-RN as a registration exam for RNs. 
 Pharmacy licensure commonly uses national certification from the Pharmacy Examining Board of Canada. 
 Physicians follow pathways supported by the Medical Council of Canada and provincial colleges; MCC describes licensure pathways that include certification recognised via CFPC/Royal College/College des medecins du Quebec routes (details vary by province). 
 The College of Family Physicians of Canada is a key certifying body for family medicine. 

Australia (national registration scheme)
Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency administers the National Registration and Accreditation Scheme with national boards for multiple professions (including nursing, medicine, dentistry, pharmacy and allied health professions). 
 Jobs and Skills Australia occupation profiles also explicitly flag where registration is required. 

UK pay reference used in profiles
For roles typically employed under NHS England terms, the key "indicative pay" reference is the 2026/27 AfC annual pay scales (entry and top step). 

For doctors, dentists, and some senior medical contracts, pay is commonly separate from AfC; consultant pay tables are published by the BMA and NHS pay awards are documented through official announcements and parliamentary briefings. 

Where a role is commonly outside AfC (e.g., private practice, contractor models, industry), pay is marked as "varies widely".

Clinical care careers
Physician / medical doctor
Typical titles: GP; hospital doctor; specialty registrar; consultant physician/surgeon; attending physician.
Core duties: Diagnose illness; manage acute and chronic conditions; prescribe and perform procedures; coordinate care with multidisciplinary teams; document and communicate risk, consent, and follow-up.
Training pathway (typical):

UK: medical degree (commonly 5-6 years) followed by a 2-year UK Foundation Programme bridging medical school and specialty/GP training. 
ROI: internship then entry to specialist training programmes; RCPI describes entry requirements including internship completion and IMC eligibility (programme lengths vary by specialty). 
US: state licensure model; pathway commonly includes USMLE-linked examination milestones and postgraduate training. 
Canada: licensure pathways involve MCC-recognised routes; provincial licensing and certification pathways vary. 
AU: national registration scheme (AHPRA); internship and specialty college training typically required for independent specialist practice (details vary by specialty). 

Licensing/registration: UK: GMC; ROI: Medical Council; US: state medical board; CA: provincial college; AU: AHPRA. 

Settings: GP practices; hospitals; urgent care; community; public health; academia; industry.
Progression/specialisms: GP; medical specialties; surgery; psychiatry; public health; clinical leadership; research.
Pay (indicative): UK/ROI/AU/CA vary strongly by grade/contract; US physician pay varies widely by specialty; median-type benchmarks are not reliably comparable across jurisdictions in a single table.
Outlook/demand: Generally high demand in most systems; US healthcare overall grows faster than average and the UK workforce plan models sustained expansion needs. 

Entry routes: Undergraduate medicine; graduate entry pathways (where available); widening participation routes; for international entrants, jurisdiction-specific recognition/exams.
Dentist
Typical titles: general dentist; specialist dentist (orthodontics, oral surgery, etc.).
Core duties: Oral examinations; diagnosis of dental disease; restorative work; extractions; prevention education; manage dental records and infection control.
Training pathway (typical): Professional dental degree + supervised practice (varies).
Licensing/registration: UK: GDC registration required and dentistry is illegal to practise without registration number; ROI: Dental Council maintains registers; US/CA/AU: state/provincial/national board registration models (vary). 

Settings: Primary dental practice; community dental services; hospitals (specialists); academia.
Progression/specialisms: Specialist lists; practice ownership; maxillofacial pathways (jurisdiction-specific).
Pay: Highly variable by contract type, private/NHS mix, and location.
Outlook: Generally resilient demand driven by ongoing oral health needs and ageing populations (jurisdiction-specific).
Entry routes: Dental degree; dental therapy/hygiene as an adjacent entry and progression route.

Dental hygienist / dental therapist
Typical titles: dental hygienist; dental therapist; dental care professional (DCP).
Core duties: Preventive care; periodontal assessment; scale/polish; oral health education; (therapist scope may include restorations under defined remit).
Training: Often diploma/degree routes (varies by country and exact scope).
Licensing/registration: UK: GDC regulates DCP titles including dental hygienist and dental therapist; ROI: Dental Council maintains the register of dental hygienists; US/CA/AU: jurisdiction-specific licensure. 

Settings: Dental practices; community; hospitals.
Progression: Extended duties; education; practice leadership.
Pay: Varies; in NHS contexts may align to AfC bands for employed roles; private practice often sessional. 

Outlook: Strong in many regions due to preventive focus and expanding scope.

Dental assistant / dental nurse
Typical titles: dental nurse; dental assistant.
Core duties: Chairside assistance; infection control; instrument preparation; patient admin; record support; imaging support depending on credentialing.
Training: Certificate/apprenticeship-style routes common; role scope depends on regulation.
Licensing/registration: UK: GDC regulates dental nurses; ROI: Dental Council register of dental nurses is stated as voluntary (but certain tasks like radiographs may require specific approved training/registration); US/CA/AU: varies. 

Settings: Dental practices; hospitals.
Progression: Senior dental nurse; radiography qualification; practice management.
Pay: Often AfC banded in NHS employed roles; otherwise varies. 

Outlook: Stable to strong with dental service demand.

Registered nurse
Typical titles: staff nurse; charge nurse; clinical nurse specialist; ward sister/charge nurse (UK terms vary).
Core duties: Assessment, planning, implementation and evaluation of nursing care; medication administration; monitoring and escalation; patient/family communication; supervision of support workers; discharge planning. 

Training (typical): UK: NMC-approved nursing programmes typically minimum 3 years (some accelerated/recognition routes exist). 
 AU: registration required with Nursing and Midwifery Board of Australia. 

Licensing/registration: UK: NMC register required; ROI: NMBI register required; US: state nursing board + NCLEX; CA: provincial regulator + entry-to-practice exam (NCLEX-RN for RNs noted by CCRNR); AU: AHPRA/NMBA registration. 

Settings: Hospitals; community; primary care; mental health; care homes; schools; occupational health.
Progression/specialisms: ICU/ED; theatres; community; mental health; leadership; advanced practice routes.
Pay (indicative): UK AfC Band 5 commonly for newly qualified roles (Band 5 range shown on 2026/27 pay scales); Australia median weekly earnings for registered nurses are published by Jobs and Skills Australia. 
 US medians vary by role and setting (see BLS nursing categories). 

Outlook: Very strong demand in most systems; US healthcare has high projected openings overall; UK workforce planning emphasises expansion/retention. 

Entry routes: Nursing degree; apprenticeship routes exist for adjacent roles (e.g., nursing associate in England and bridging routes).

Licensed practical/vocational nurse / enrolled nurse / nursing associate
Typical titles: LPN/LVN (US); enrolled nurse (AU); nursing associate (England).
Core duties: Basic nursing care under RN/physician supervision; vital signs; wound care; documentation; patient support.
Training: Often post-secondary certificate/diploma routes (varies). Nursing associate programmes in England are explicitly regulated through NMC registration. 

Licensing/registration: UK: NMC (for nursing associates in England); ROI: NMBI divisions (role names differ); US: state board + NCLEX-PN; CA/AU: provincial/state/board model (varies by designation). 

Settings: Hospitals; care homes; community.
Progression: Bridge to RN; specialist support roles.
Pay: UK often AfC Band 3-4 depending role; varies elsewhere. 

Outlook: Strong demand, especially in long-term care and community settings. 

Midwife
Typical titles: midwife; community midwife; consultant midwife (UK).
Core duties: Antenatal, intrapartum and postnatal care; safeguarding; education; emergency escalation; multidisciplinary coordination.
Training: Degree programme routes; some jurisdictions have direct-entry midwifery and/or RN-to-midwife pathways.
Licensing/registration: UK: NMC regulates midwives; ROI: NMBI regulates midwives; US/CA/AU vary (US includes certified nurse-midwife and other models; AU includes Nursing and Midwifery Board registration under AHPRA). 

Settings: Hospitals; birthing centres; community.
Progression: Specialist midwife; education; leadership.
Pay: UK typically AfC banded for employed roles; varies elsewhere. 

Outlook: Generally strong demand where birth services require staffing.

Nurse practitioner / advanced practice nursing (including nurse anaesthetist and nurse midwife models)
Typical titles: nurse practitioner; advanced nurse practitioner; nurse anaesthetist (US); nurse midwife (US).
Core duties: Advanced assessment; diagnosis; prescribing (jurisdiction-dependent); independent or semi-independent clinics; complex case management.
Training: Postgraduate education is typical; BLS groups nurse anesthetists, nurse midwives and nurse practitioners with master's entry-level education. 

Licensing/registration: UK: advanced practice is often a role level rather than separate licence (but base NMC registration applies; prescribing requires additional credentialing); US: state APRN licensure/certification model varies; AU: nurse practitioner is a specific category (see Jobs and Skills profile linkouts) under national registration. 

Settings: Primary care; hospitals; specialist clinics; anaesthesia services (US).
Progression: Clinical leadership; service development.
Pay: Higher than base RN in many systems but varies by jurisdiction and contract.
Outlook: Strong where access pressures drive expanded scopes. 

Physician assistant / physician associate
Typical titles: physician assistant (US); physician associate (UK usage).
Core duties: Medical history and exam; diagnostics; treatment plans; procedures within delegated scope; continuity clinics.
Training: Commonly master's degree programmes (US BLS entry-level education for PAs is master's). 

Licensing/registration: UK: GMC registers physician associates; regulation expanded from 12/2024. 
 US: state licensure; certification commonly via NCCPA (PANCE). 
 Canada/Australia: role existence and regulation vary by province/state and health system.
Settings: Hospitals; primary care; specialties.
Progression: Specialty PA; leadership; education.
Pay: Varies widely by country and scope.
Outlook: Often strong where systems use mid-level clinician models to expand capacity (politically and professionally variable).

Pharmacist
Typical titles: clinical pharmacist; community pharmacist; hospital pharmacist; prescribing pharmacist (where enabled).
Core duties: Dispense and optimise medicines; clinical checks; patient counselling; medicines reconciliation; stewardship; vaccination services (jurisdiction-dependent).
Training: UK route commonly includes a GPhC-approved MPharm (4 years) + 1 year foundation training + registration assessment. 

Licensing/registration: UK (GB): GPhC; ROI: PSI; US: state board of pharmacy; CA: PEBC certification + provincial registration; AU: AHPRA-linked national boards. 

Settings: Community pharmacies; hospitals; primary care networks; industry; regulators.
Progression/specialisms: Clinical specialisation; independent prescribing (UK); management; industry.
Pay: UK often AfC banded in hospital roles (commonly Band 6+ for early clinical roles, varies); community varies; ROI/US/CA/AU vary. 

Outlook: Generally stable to strong with medicines optimisation and ageing populations.

Pharmacy technician
Typical titles: pharmacy technician; medicines management technician.
Core duties: Support dispensing; accuracy checking (where trained); stock control; ward-based medicines management; controlled drug processes within governance.
Training: UK: to practise as a pharmacy technician in Great Britain you need to register with GPhC and meet its education/training requirements. 

Licensing/registration: UK (GB): GPhC registration; ROI: terminology differs (PSI registers "pharmaceutical assistants"); CA: PEBC certification also covers pharmacy technicians; AU/US: varies by jurisdiction. 

Settings: Hospital pharmacies; community pharmacies; primary care.
Progression: Senior technician; accuracy checking; management.
Pay: UK often AfC Band 4-5; varies elsewhere. 

Outlook: Strong where medicines complexity and safety requirements grow.

Optometrist
Typical titles: optometrist; clinician optometrist; independent prescriber optometrist (where enabled).
Core duties: Eye examinations; detect/manage ocular disease; prescribe corrective lenses; refer to ophthalmology; deliver eye health services.
Licensing/registration: UK: GOC; ROI: CORU includes optometrists in its regulated professions list; US/CA/AU: jurisdiction-specific licensing/national boards. 

Settings: High street optics; clinics; hospitals (specialist).
Progression: Specialty practice; prescribing; management.
Pay: Highly variable (employment vs practice ownership; public vs private).
Outlook: Stable with growing eye health demand.

Dispensing optician / optician
Typical titles: dispensing optician; optician; optical dispenser.
Core duties: Fit lenses/frames; advise on vision correction; measurements; customer/patient support; liaise with optometrists.
Licensing/registration: UK: GOC holds registers for dispensing opticians; Ireland: CORU includes dispensing opticians; US/CA/AU: regulation varies. 

Settings: Optical practices; clinics.
Progression: Practice management; specialist dispensing.
Pay: Variable.

Paramedic / EMT
Typical titles: paramedic; advanced paramedic; EMT (US).
Core duties: Pre-hospital assessment and treatment; emergency response and triage; transport decisions; documentation; liaison with ED and community services.
Training: UK degree apprenticeship routes are typically 3 years for Level 6 paramedic apprenticeships (other routes exist); HCPC registration required to use protected title. 

Licensing/registration: UK: HCPC; ROI: CORU includes radiographers and other AHPs--paramedic regulation differs and should be checked per Irish framework; US: state EMS licensing varies; CA/AU: varies (AU has different state models and increasing professionalisation). 

Settings: Ambulance services; ED; urgent care; community response.
Progression: Advanced practice; critical care paramedic; leadership.
Pay: UK often AfC Band 5-6 depending role; varies elsewhere. 

Outlook: Strong due to urgent care demand. 

Chiropractor
Typical titles: chiropractor; sports chiropractor (practice-specific).
Core duties: Assess and treat musculoskeletal conditions, often spinal manipulation and related therapies; maintain records; patient education.
Licensing/registration: UK: the profession is regulated and all chiropractors must be registered with the GCC; the title is protected. 
 Other countries: licensure varies but is typically regulated at state/provincial levels where recognised.
Settings: Private practice; multidisciplinary MSK clinics.
Progression: Practice ownership; speciality interests.
Pay: Variable (mostly private).

Osteopath
Typical titles: osteopath.
Core duties: Diagnosis and manual therapy for musculoskeletal issues; patient education; referral as needed.
Licensing/registration: UK: by law osteopaths must be registered with the GOsC to practise. 
 Other countries: regulation varies.
Settings: Private clinics; multidisciplinary practices.
Progression: Practice ownership; education; research.
Pay: Variable (often private).

Allied health and rehabilitation careers
Physiotherapist / physical therapist
Typical titles: physiotherapist; MSK physiotherapist; neuro physiotherapist; respiratory physio; advanced practice physio.
Core duties: Assess movement/function; deliver rehab plans; exercise prescription; pain management; discharge planning; prevention and self-management.
Training: UK professional body guidance notes degrees typically 3 years full-time (4 in Scotland) and pre-registration master's routes exist; registration with HCPC required to practise as a physiotherapist/physical therapist in the UK. 

Licensing/registration: UK: HCPC; ROI: CORU includes physiotherapists; US: state licensure; CA/AU: provincial/national board models. 

Settings: Hospitals; community; outpatient; private practice; sports.
Progression/specialisms: Advanced practice; specialist clinical pathways; leadership; research.
Pay: UK commonly AfC Band 5-7 depending seniority; US median pay varies by state and setting; other regions vary. 

Outlook: Strong, especially in ageing, MSK and post-acute demand. 

Occupational therapist
Typical titles: occupational therapist; OT; paediatric OT; mental health OT.
Core duties: Enable independence in activities of daily living; adaptive equipment; cognitive and functional assessment; home/environmental assessment; rehab planning.
Training: Commonly master's level entry in US; UK is typically undergraduate or pre-registration master's routes with HCPC registration. 

Licensing/registration: UK: HCPC; ROI: CORU includes OTs; US: state (plus NBCOT exam in practice, not detailed here); CA/AU: regulated by provincial/national boards. 

Settings: Hospitals; community; social care; schools; mental health services.
Progression: Specialist OT; leadership; private practice.
Pay: UK commonly AfC Band 5-7; varies globally. 

Outlook: Strong where rehab and community care expand. 

Speech and language therapist / speech-language pathologist (SLT/SLP)
Typical titles: speech and language therapist; speech-language pathologist; paediatric SLT.
Core duties: Assess and treat communication, speech, language and swallowing disorders; therapy programmes; work with schools, stroke services, ENT, neurorehab.
Training: Commonly master's entry in US; UK routes include degree pathways with HCPC registration required for protected title. 

Licensing/registration: UK: HCPC; ROI: CORU includes SLTs; US/CA/AU: licensure varies. 

Settings: Hospitals; community; schools; private practice.
Progression: Specialist SLT; leadership; research.
Pay: UK typically AfC Band 5-7; varies globally. 

Outlook: Strong in paeds, neuro, dysphagia services. 

Dietitian / nutritionist
Typical titles: dietitian; specialist dietitian (renal, diabetes, ICU); public health dietitian.
Core duties: Nutritional assessment; care plans; therapeutic diets; enteral feeding support; education; multidisciplinary working.
Licensing/registration: UK: "dietitian" is an HCPC protected title; ROI: CORU includes dietitians; US/CA/AU vary. 

Settings: Hospitals; community; outpatient; industry; sports.
Progression: Specialist dietetics; management; research.
Pay: UK often AfC Band 5-7; varies elsewhere. 

Outlook: Strong in chronic disease and prevention. 

Audiologist and hearing aid dispenser
Typical titles: audiologist; clinical audiologist; hearing aid dispenser (UK protected title under HCPC).
Core duties: Hearing assessment; device fitting; rehabilitation; balance testing (scope varies); patient counselling.
Regulation: UK: hearing aid dispensers are within HCPC's regulated professions list; other hearing roles may be regulated via different routes depending on title. 
 US: audiologists are a doctoral/professional degree role per BLS; regulation varies by state. 

Settings: Hospitals; clinics; community; commercial hearing services.
Progression: Specialist clinics; management.
Pay: UK often AfC for NHS roles; commercial varies. 

Podiatrist / chiropodist
Typical titles: podiatrist; chiropodist; high-risk foot specialist.
Core duties: Foot/ankle assessment; ulcer prevention; diabetic foot care; biomechanics; nail/skin conditions.
Licensing/registration: UK: chiropodist/podiatrist is an HCPC protected title; ROI: CORU includes podiatrists/chiropodists; US has podiatrists as doctoral/professional degree; AU regulated under national scheme. 

Settings: Community; hospitals; private practice.
Progression: Advanced practice; surgery (jurisdiction-specific).
Pay: UK often AfC; private varies. 

Orthotist / prosthetist
Typical titles: prosthetist; orthotist; prosthetist-orthotist.
Core duties: Design/fit orthoses and prostheses; gait assessment; patient training; coordination with rehab teams.
Licensing/registration: UK: prosthetists/orthotists are HCPC-regulated; ROI: not explicitly listed on the CORU snapshot above (verify current register status by profession); US BLS notes master's entry-level education for orthotists/prosthetists; AU regulation varies by board/occupation. 

Settings: Hospitals; rehab centres; specialist limb services.
Progression: Specialist upper-limb/lower-limb; leadership.
Pay: UK often AfC Band 5-7; varies elsewhere. 

Therapy assistants (physiotherapy assistant / occupational therapy assistant)
Typical titles: therapy assistant; rehabilitation assistant; OT assistant (US regulated differently); PTA/OTA (US).
Core duties: Deliver delegated rehab activities; prepare treatment areas; record observations; support mobility and ADLs; patient motivation.
Training: Certificate to associate degree depending on country and role. 

Licensing/registration: UK: usually not statutorily regulated (unless using protected titles); US: OT/PT assistants have specific credentialing; CA/AU varies.
Settings: Hospitals; community rehab; care homes.
Progression: Senior assistant; assistant practitioner; degree entry routes.

Diagnostics and healthcare science careers
Radiographer / radiologic technologist (including MRI)
Typical titles: diagnostic radiographer; radiologic technologist; MRI technologist.
Core duties: Imaging acquisition; radiation safety; patient positioning; contrast administration (credentialed); quality assurance; reporting support (where advanced practice).
Training: UK National Careers guidance indicates degree or postgraduate course approved by HCPC is required for radiography; registration required. 

Licensing/registration: UK: HCPC; ROI: CORU includes radiographers and radiation therapists; US/CA/AU: licensure varies. 

Settings: Hospitals; community diagnostic centres; private imaging.
Progression: Reporting radiographer/advanced practice; management; modalities specialisation.
Pay: UK commonly AfC Band 5-7; varies elsewhere. 

Outlook: Strong with imaging demand and service expansion. 

Therapeutic radiographer / radiation therapist
Typical titles: therapeutic radiographer; radiation therapist.
Core duties: Radiotherapy planning/delivery; patient setup; safety checks; support during treatment; multidisciplinary cancer care.
Licensing/registration: UK: HCPC (radiographer titles); ROI: CORU includes radiation therapists; AU/US/CA vary. 

Settings: Cancer centres; hospitals.
Progression: Advanced practice; dosimetry/planning leadership; management.
Pay: UK typically AfC; varies elsewhere. 

Diagnostic medical sonographer
Typical titles: sonographer; ultrasound technologist.
Core duties: Ultrasound imaging; measurements; preliminary findings; patient care during procedures.
Training: Often associate degree in US per BLS; in the UK, "sonographer" as a title is not uniformly regulated--commonly mapped through a regulated base profession (e.g., radiography) plus postgraduate competency (varies by employer). 

Licensing/registration: US: varies; UK: check base profession and role requirements; AU/CA: varies.
Settings: Hospitals; maternity; outpatient imaging.

Nuclear medicine technologist
Typical titles: nuclear medicine technologist.
Core duties: Radiopharmaceutical prep/admin (credentialed); imaging; radiation safety; patient monitoring.
Training: US BLS notes associate degree typical entry. 

Licensing/registration: UK: often through HCPC as radiographer/clinical scientist routes depending on role; US/CA/AU vary. 

Settings: Hospitals; imaging centres.

Medical dosimetrist
Typical titles: medical dosimetrist.
Core duties: Calculate radiation dose; design radiotherapy treatment plans; quality checks with physicists and oncologists.
Training: US BLS lists bachelor's entry education for medical dosimetrists. 

Licensing/registration: Varies; often credentialed via professional certification (jurisdiction-dependent).
Settings: Radiotherapy departments.

Biomedical scientist / clinical laboratory technologist
Typical titles: biomedical scientist (UK); medical laboratory scientist/technologist.
Core duties: Laboratory testing (haematology, biochemistry, microbiology); quality control; interpret results within scope; lab safety; sample chain-of-custody.
Licensing/registration: UK: biomedical scientists are listed among HCPC-regulated professions with protected titles; ROI: CORU includes medical scientists; US/CA/AU vary. 

Settings: Hospital labs; public health labs; blood services; industry.
Progression: Specialist scientist; lab management; quality lead.
Pay: UK often AfC Band 5-7; varies elsewhere. 

Outlook: Strong due to diagnostics expansion. 

Clinical scientist (NHS Scientist Training Programme model)
Typical titles: clinical scientist; consultant clinical scientist; specialty clinical scientist (e.g., genomics, medical physics, clinical engineering).
Core duties: Advanced diagnostics; interpretation; specialised clinical services; quality and safety governance; innovation and research translation.
Training: The National School of Healthcare Science describes the Scientist Training Programme (STP) as a 3-year work-based programme supported by a master's degree. 

Registration: UK: clinical scientist is an HCPC protected title; registration typically follows approved training or equivalence routes (AHCS supports equivalence pathways). 
 ROI/AU/US/CA: comparable senior scientist roles exist but regulation differs.
Settings: NHS specialist services; labs; imaging/physics; genomics hubs.
Progression: Consultant scientist; service lead; academia/industry.
Pay: UK commonly AfC Band 7+ (often via specialist scientist pathways); varies elsewhere. 

Medical physicist
Typical titles: medical physicist; clinical scientist (medical physics).
Core duties: Radiation safety; equipment quality assurance; dosimetry; imaging optimisation; treatment planning support; regulatory compliance.
Training/registration: Often via clinical scientist pathways; in the UK, clinical scientist is HCPC protected title. 

Settings: Radiotherapy; radiology; nuclear medicine.
Progression: Leadership; R&D.

Respiratory therapist
Typical titles: respiratory therapist (US/Canada); pulmonary therapist.
Core duties: Ventilation support; respiratory assessments; pulmonary rehab support; airway management assistance; ICU/ED respiratory care.
Training: US BLS lists associate degree entry for respiratory therapists. 

Licensing: Strongly jurisdiction-dependent; role is less standardised in UK (often covered by physiotherapy/clinical physiology/nursing).
Settings: Hospitals; ICU; sleep labs.

Public health and prevention careers
Public health specialist / consultant
Typical titles: consultant in public health; public health specialist; health protection specialist (titles vary).
Core duties: Population health assessment; programme design; outbreak response; policy advice; data interpretation; commissioning and evaluation.
Training: Highly variable--medical and non-medical consultant routes exist with postgraduate specialty training pathways (jurisdiction-specific).

Source notes, limitations, and where details vary widely
No single regulator or government source publishes a globally exhaustive list of "all health and medicine careers" with consistent job titles, duties, training, licensure and pay across the UK/ROI/US/CA/AU--because titles and scopes are defined by different legal frameworks, education systems, and employers. As a result, this report takes an occupational family approach and uses primary/official sources to anchor three critical dimensions: (1) statutory regulation/protected titles, (2) training pathway descriptions where official, and (3) pay frameworks where published. 

Where information is explicitly variable, the report marks it as such:

Counsellor/psychotherapist regulation: Ireland lists counsellors and psychotherapists among professions within CORU legislation; practical register status and protected title enforcement can differ by profession and timing--this must be verified against the current register status for the profession in question. 
Sonographer and some informatics titles: often not protected as a single statutory title in the UK; employers commonly require a regulated base profession and defined competencies.
Pay comparability: NHS AfC provides an authoritative public sector band reference for many UK roles, but many roles are outside AfC (medical contracts, dentistry, private practice, industry). Similarly, US BLS medians are robust within the US but not directly comparable to UK/ROI/AU public scales.
