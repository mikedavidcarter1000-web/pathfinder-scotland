
Executive Summary
Business & Finance offers a wide spectrum of careers--from traditional accounting and banking roles to specialist fields like actuarial science, finance, insurance and corporate governance. Core functions include managing money, advising businesses or individuals, analysing markets and ensuring legal/regulatory compliance. Specialist roles (e.g. actuaries, compliance officers) apply advanced technical skills to niche problems. Entry routes vary: many professions require academic degrees (often in finance, economics, maths or business), professional qualifications (ACCA/CIMA/ICAEW for accountants, FCA-regulated exams for financial advisers, IFoA exams for actuaries, CII for insurers, CIPS for procurement, CIPD for HR), or apprenticeships and vocational training
. Workplaces range from offices (e.g. banks, finance departments, insurance firms) to field roles (e.g. insurance surveyors visiting sites). Salaries typically rise steeply with experience: for example, graduate accounting/finance roles start around GBP 20-25k, mid-career managers reach GBP 50-70k, and top specialists (e.g. CFOs, senior consultants) can exceed six figures. Career progression often follows structured paths: for example, analysts -> senior analysts/manager -> director, or trainee accountant -> chartered accountant -> finance director
. Key competencies include numerical and analytical ability, communication, IT proficiency and business acumen. Many roles also demand resilience and ethical judgement under pressure.

Context: UK (and Scottish) regulation shapes many professions. E.g. financial advisers and mortgage brokers must be FCA-authorised and hold certified diplomas; solicitors/CIPD-defined HR roles must meet professional standards; accountants often pursue chartered status through bodies like ACCA/CIMA/ICAEW; actuaries train via the IFoA. In Scotland, vocational routes (Foundation/Modern Apprenticeships, HNDs, SVQs) are widely used
. We emphasise accurate compliance with UK/Scottish standards, and realistic workload pressures (health/safety considerations, peak-season workloads, regulatory audits).

This report presents detailed job profiles for the main Business & Finance careers. It tabulates each role's definition, tasks, entry requirements and qualifications (academic and professional), regulatory/licensing bodies, typical employers/environments, salary bands, progression path, core skills, workload/pressure and common misconceptions. Following the matrix, we give career-path flowcharts for six representative roles, and conclude with recommendations for students choosing between these careers.

Recommendations for Students
Match Interests & Strengths: Accountancy and actuarial roles suit those strong in maths and detail; marketing/sales roles suit those with creativity and people skills; HR suits those interested in people and employment law; investment/banking suit ambitious, high-pressure environments; procurement suits organised negotiators; economist/analyst suits data-oriented thinkers. Reflect on your interests.
Qualifications: Many finance roles require degrees (often 2:1+) and relevant A-levels (Maths, Economics). Apprenticeships exist for entry roles (e.g. finance administrator, accounting technician, procurement administrator). Professional exams (e.g. ACCA, CIMA, FCA diplomas) are crucial - look up entry requirements on bodies' sites (ACCA, CII, CIPD, CIPS, IFoA). In Scotland, consider foundation apprenticeships (Financial Services FA) and HND pathways
.
Accreditations & Regulation: Be aware of regulations: financial advisers and mortgage brokers need FCA approval and exams; accountants usually seek chartered status; HR professionals typically join CIPD; procurement specialists often join CIPS. Research each profession's regulatory bodies early.
Experience: Seek internships or part-time work (e.g. in banking, insurance, government departments). Many firms offer graduate schemes and vacation work. For finance, software skills (Excel, SQL) and certifications (e.g. CII certificates, CISI) can improve employability.
Career Planning: Use mentors and careers services (e.g. My World of Work in Scotland, university careers) to compare pathways. Consider the workload: e.g. finance year-end peaks, exam commitments. Explore "like this career" links from NCS or SDS to discover related roles.
Continuous Learning: All fields evolve (e.g. fintech in banking, IR35 in taxation, GDPR in data roles). Stay informed of UK and Scottish legal changes. Membership in professional bodies offers updates and networking (e.g. Chartered Banker Institute, PMI, ACCA, etc.).
Students should weigh long-term interests (finance vs. strategy vs. people) and lifestyle. For instance, investment banking demands long hours and travel, whereas corporate finance roles may offer better work-life balance. Always clarify entry requirements with universities or vocational bodies before committing. Where unsure, consider work-shadowing or short courses to test a field.
