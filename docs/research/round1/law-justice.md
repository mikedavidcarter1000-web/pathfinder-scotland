
Careers in Law and Justice in the UK
Executive summary
The law-and-justice labour market in the United Kingdom spans regulated legal professions (e.g., solicitors, barristers/advocates, legal executives), judicial and court roles (judges, magistrates, tribunal members, court legal advisers, court staff), policing and border enforcement, prisons/probation, forensic and digital investigation, legal reform and policy, corporate compliance, and victim/support and dispute-resolution services. 

Entry routes vary sharply by sector. Regulated legal practice typically requires passing defined qualification stages and meeting "character and suitability" standards (e.g., solicitor admission requirements and barrister practising authorisation), while many justice-system operational roles recruit through structured on-the-job training pipelines (e.g., policing entry pathways; prison and probation recruitment). 

Pay and conditions can range from volunteer/expense-only (e.g., magistrates) through mid-career public service bands (courts, probation, policing) up to high-earning specialist advocacy and senior in-house leadership (e.g., senior barristers; general counsel), with substantial variation by region, seniority, practice area, and whether work is employed or self-employed. 

Stress, risk and wellbeing factors are material in most pathways. Frontline enforcement and custodial roles can involve shift work, confrontation risk and exposure to traumatic material; legal practice can involve high workload pressure, tight deadlines, and emotionally demanding client matters; and investigative roles demand rigorous evidence handling and professional standards that directly affect justice outcomes. 

Scope, definitions and salary assumptions
This report provides a UK-focused, cross-sector map of careers in "Law and Justice" covering public, private and non-profit settings. It is comprehensive in coverage of major role families and commonly recognised titles, but it cannot be a literal inventory of every job title used by every employer (the sector is too large and titles vary). Where a title is a specialisation rather than a legally distinct profession (e.g., "tax lawyer"), it is treated as a career variant with the same core qualification route as a solicitor/barrister unless specified otherwise. 

Jurisdiction matters. Many legal roles are separately regulated and trained across England & Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland (e.g., the advocate/barrister split differs; solicitor regulation differs; some reciprocal admission arrangements differ). 

Salary bands are stated in GBP /year and, unless noted, assume UK full-time employment and are drawn primarily from the government-backed National Careers Service job profiles (or other official/professional-body sources where a role is not covered). For self-employed and fee-paid roles (common in advocacy, mediation/arbitration, some tribunal appointments, and private investigation), earnings are often variable and may be better thought of as a range of outcomes rather than a salary. 

All information is current as of 14/04/2026 (Europe/London). Where an official source does not provide salary data, the salary is shown as unspecified/variable rather than estimated.

Career profiles by sector
Legal practice and legal services

Solicitor (England & Wales)
Role definition & duties: Advises clients on the law and acts on their behalf (advisory work, drafting, negotiation, case preparation, and managing legal processes). Typical tasks include instructing barristers, drafting legal documents, researching case law, and attending meetings/tribunals/negotiations. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Common modern route is via Solicitors Regulation Authority requirements (SQE assessments + at least 2 years' qualifying work experience + character and suitability), with transitional routes also described by SRA. 

Salary & progression: Typical UK salary band stated as GBP 30,000-GBP 80,000; progression can include becoming a partner in private practice or managing an in-house legal team, and specialising in a practice area. 

Employers & settings: Law firms (high street to international), in-house legal teams, government departments and agencies, local authorities; settings can include offices, courts, prisons and police stations depending on practice area. 

Key skills: Legal analysis, drafting, client communication, organisation, resilience under pressure, and digital competence. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Typical hours 37-50/week, including potential on-call/short-notice work; stress often comes from high stakes, client vulnerability, deadlines, and adversarial disputes. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: In-house counsel, legal aid solicitor, Crown prosecutor, compliance roles (e.g., COLP), or specialisms such as family, immigration, human rights, IP, tax, environmental, and cyber/privacy. 

Official sources: National Careers Service (solicitor profile); SRA SQE/QWE/admission guidance; professional context from The Law Society of England and Wales. 

Barrister (England & Wales)
Role definition & duties: Provides specialist legal advice and represents clients (often via solicitors) in courts/tribunals and dispute hearings, including advocacy and written submissions. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Regulated by Bar Standards Board; practising requires authorisation and a practising certificate for reserved legal activities, with training structured through Bar qualification stages and pupillage requirements described by the BSB. 

Salary & progression: Typical salary band shown as GBP 24,000-GBP 250,000, with very wide dispersion by seniority, practice area, and whether employed vs self-employed. 

Employers & settings: Often self-employed at chambers (supported by clerks), or employed by organisations such as the CPS or government legal teams; work is court-centred and deadline-driven. 

Key skills: Oral advocacy, strategic thinking, legal research and writing, confidence under scrutiny, ethical judgement, and time management. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Typical hours 40-48/week, with variable workload peaks around trials and hearings; income volatility can be a major stressor for self-employed practice. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Crown Prosecution advocacy, specialist areas (crime, public law/human rights, IP, family, commercial), or later judicial appointment routes. 

Official sources: National Careers Service (barrister profile) and BSB practising/qualification materials. 

Advocate (Scotland)
Role definition & duties: The Faculty of Advocates describes advocates as independent specialist court lawyers providing representation and advice (rights of audience across courts). Their training includes "devilling" (Scottish pupillage-equivalent) and assessments. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Government regulated professions information summarises that qualification generally includes a Scots law degree, Diploma in Legal Practice, specified subject passes, evidence/practice/procedure examination, periods of training in a solicitor's office, and pupillage/devilling with assessment under the Faculty's scheme. 

Salary & progression: Salary is variable/unspecified in the primary sources used here (advocates are commonly self-employed and earnings vary by practice and seniority). 

Employers & settings: Typically self-employed and instructed by solicitors; work is heavily court-based with significant preparation time. 

Key skills: Advocacy, legal analysis, independence, professionalism, and resilience under adversarial pressure. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Peaks around hearings; high performance pressure and income variability are common risk factors for self-employed practice. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Specialist advocacy (criminal, civil, public law), solicitor-advocate routes, academic practice, or judicial office. 

Official sources: Regulated Professions Register (advocate) and Faculty of Advocates devilling guidance. 

Chartered Legal Executive / CILEX Lawyer (England & Wales)
Role definition & duties: Legal executives specialise in areas of law and carry out tasks similar to solicitors (advising, document preparation, dispute work). 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Multiple routes, including CILEX pathways (including CPQ) and qualifying employment; authorisation and reserved activities are governed through CILEx Regulation practising certificates and rights frameworks. 

Salary & progression: Typical salary band GBP 33,000-GBP 60,000; progression can include specialising further, practice rights (litigation/advocacy/immigration, etc.), senior fee-earner roles, partnership equivalents, or judicial pathways. 

Employers & settings: Law firms, corporate legal departments, and government legal teams; typically office-based with court work in litigation-heavy specialisms. 

Key skills: Legal drafting, client management, attention to detail, and practice-area depth. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Typical hours 39-42/week; workload pressure rises in litigation deadlines and transactions. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Solicitor qualification via SQE routes for CILEX practitioners, costs law, conveyancing, immigration advice, or compliance. 

Official sources: National Careers Service (legal executive profile); CILEx Regulation rights/responsibilities; CILEX qualification guidance. 

Paralegal
Role definition & duties: Conducts legal research, prepares documents, manages case information, interviews clients/witnesses, and may attend court under supervision; often supports solicitors and legal teams. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Entry routes include legal apprenticeships, legal services T Levels/college routes, degrees, and on-the-job progression; professional development may align with CILEX pathways or firm-based training (not a single mandatory regulator for paralegals). 

Salary & progression: Typical salary band GBP 20,000-GBP 40,000; progression commonly into senior paralegal/case handler roles, CILEX qualification, or SQE qualifying work experience toward solicitor qualification. 

Employers & settings: Law firms, in-house teams, the public sector (e.g., prosecution/courts), and charities providing advice; work is mainly office-based with possible court/police-station exposure. 

Key skills: Organisation, legal research, confidentiality handling, drafting, and client communication. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Typical hours 37-40/week, with peaks around filings/hearings; stress from deadlines, high-volume caseloads, and client vulnerability. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Legal assistant, litigation assistant, conveyancing case handler, compliance paralegal, or legal executive track. 

Official sources: National Careers Service (paralegal profile). 

Legal Secretary / Legal PA
Role definition & duties: Provides administrative support (document production, diary/case management, correspondence handling, client liaison) for solicitors and legal executives. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Often enters via admin qualifications, legal secretary apprenticeships/college routes, and on-the-job training (no single professional registration). 

Salary & progression: Typical salary band GBP 24,000-GBP 40,000; progression to senior legal PA, team leader, office manager, or transition into paralegal work. 

Employers & settings: Private law firms, barristers' chambers, in-house legal departments, local authorities. 

Key skills: Accuracy, confidentiality, typing/document systems, workflow coordination, and client-facing professionalism. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Typical hours 36-40/week, generally office hours but with deadline peaks; stress tends to be deadline and volume driven. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Legal operations, paralegal, court administration, compliance admin, or casework support roles. 

Official sources: National Careers Service (legal secretary profile). 

Barrister's Clerk / Chambers Professional
Role definition & duties: Runs chambers administration and practice management (diary allocation, fee negotiation, client liaison, business development, and coordinating work flow for barristers). 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Typically enters through administrative/graduate routes with chambers training; professional development is often sector-led rather than a statutory licence. 

Salary & progression: Typical salary band GBP 20,000-GBP 40,000; progression to senior clerk/chambers director/practice manager roles. 

Employers & settings: Barristers' chambers; frequent court interface. 

Key skills: Commercial awareness, negotiation, scheduling, stakeholder management, and discretion. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Typical hours 39-41/week, with peaks around urgent listings and trials. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Legal operations manager, litigation support manager, or business development roles within legal services. 

Official sources: National Careers Service (barrister's clerk profile). 

Licensed Conveyancer
Role definition & duties: Specialist property lawyer handling legal administration of residential/commercial purchases, sales and transfers; regulated by the conveyancing profession's regulator. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Qualification routes include professional qualifications approved by Council for Licensed Conveyancers (CLC), apprenticeships, and work-based progression. 

Salary & progression: Salary is unspecified in the primary NCS snippet used here; progression commonly includes senior conveyancer, practice manager, or running a conveyancing business. 

Employers & settings: Conveyancing firms, property law departments, some in-house property teams. 

Key skills: Detail, client communication, transaction management, land/property law competence. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Office-based; stress peaks around completions, chains, and deadlines. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Conveyancing paralegal/case handler, property solicitor route, land registration roles. 

Official sources: National Careers Service (licensed conveyancer profile) and CLC role guidance. 

Notary Public (England & Wales)
Role definition & duties: Specialist lawyer who authenticates/attests documents for use abroad; being a notary is a separate profession from being a solicitor. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Regulated through Faculty Office of the Archbishop of Canterbury (Master of the Faculties), with qualification governed by Notaries (Qualification) Rules and notarial practice course arrangements described in official registers. 

Salary & progression: Unspecified in the primary official sources used here (earnings vary by client base, locality, and whether combined with solicitor practice). 

Employers & settings: Often small practices, combined solicitor-notary services, or specialist international/document services. 

Key skills: Precision, document handling, cross-border requirements awareness, client service. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Typically office appointments; stress arises from high accuracy requirements and time-critical transactions. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: International private client, corporate notarisation, apostille/legalisation workflows. 

Official sources: Faculty Office rules and GOV.UK Regulated Professions Register (notary public). 

Patent Attorney
Role definition & duties: Advises on protecting inventions and drafts/prosecutes patent applications; can progress into partnership or move into patent-examiner roles. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Regulated by Intellectual Property Regulation Board (IPReg); entry typically expects strong STEM background and structured training toward qualification (described by professional bodies). 

Salary & progression: NCS notes progression pathways but does not provide salary in the snippet used here; other sources show wide ranges by qualification status--treat salary as unspecified in official job profile used here. 

Employers & settings: Patent attorney firms, IP boutiques, R&D-heavy corporates; can liaise with the UK Intellectual Property Office ecosystem. 

Key skills: Technical comprehension, drafting, persuasion, attention to detail, client management. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Office-based; deadline pressure around filings/oppositions; stress arises from precision and complex technical/legal argumentation. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Trade mark attorney dual-qualification, IP solicitor/barrister, patent examiner. 

Official sources: National Careers Service (patent attorney profile) and IPReg qualification guidance; professional route information from Chartered Institute of Patent Attorneys. 

Trade Mark Attorney
Role definition & duties: Advises on brand protection (trade marks, logos, packaging) including registration strategy and enforcement support. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Qualification overseen by IPReg; professional route described by Chartered Institute of Trade Mark Attorneys. 

Salary & progression: Typical salary band GBP 30,000-GBP 90,000 per NCS; progression into senior associate/partner, in-house brand counsel, or broader IP work. 

Employers & settings: IP firms, specialist trade mark practices, in-house brand/legal teams. 

Key skills: Brand strategy understanding, written advocacy, negotiation, precision. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Office-based; workload peaks around filing deadlines and disputes. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: IP solicitor, IP barrister, patent attorney cross-training. 

Official sources: National Careers Service (trade mark attorney profile), IPReg, and CITMA qualification guidance. 

Costs Lawyer
Role definition & duties: Qualified expert in legal costs (litigation and legal aid costs), with rights to appear in court on costs matters. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Qualification delivered via accredited training providers and regulated by Costs Lawyer Standards Board (CLSB). Practising requires meeting practising rules and holding/renewing practising certificates per CLSB rules and GOV.UK regulated professions information. 

Salary & progression: Salary is unspecified in the CLSB primary sources used here (varies by employer, region and caseload complexity). 

Employers & settings: Specialist costs firms, litigation departments, insurer panels, legal aid cost specialists. 

Key skills: Detailed analysis, procedural rules competence, negotiation, and courtroom cost advocacy. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Deadline-driven (budgets, assessments, hearings); stress arises from precision and adversarial negotiation. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Costs draftsman (unregulated), litigation support, legal aid billing specialist. 

Official sources: CLSB qualification/practising guidance and GOV.UK regulated professions entry. 

Legal Aid Lawyer (criminal/civil legal aid practitioner)
Role definition & duties: A solicitor/barrister (or appropriately authorised lawyer) providing state-funded advice/representation under legal aid contracts, often for vulnerable clients in high-stakes matters. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Core qualification is solicitor/barrister route; provision of legal aid services depends on contracts administered by the Legal Aid Agency (LAA), with contracts and application guidance provided by the LAA and professional bodies. 

Salary & progression: Unspecified in primary official sources used here (varies widely by organisation type, contract category, and region). 

Employers & settings: High-street legal aid firms, not-for-profit advice agencies with contracts, specialist legal charities; work includes courts, client sites and outreach. 

Key skills: Client care, trauma-informed communication, case preparation, ethical judgement, and resilience. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Often deadline and court-timetable driven; emotional load is common due to client vulnerability and crisis situations. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Duty solicitor work, housing/benefits/public law, immigration/asylum, family/domestic abuse. 

Official sources: LAA organisation and contract documentation; Law Society guidance on tendering. 

Crown Prosecutor / Prosecuting Lawyer
Role definition & duties: Decides whether there is sufficient evidence and public interest to prosecute (charging decisions/reviewing), and prepares/prosecutes cases through court. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Typically qualified solicitor/barrister; public prosecution careers and trainee routes are described by the Crown Prosecution Service and legal trainee information. 

Salary & progression: Typical salary band GBP 41,000-GBP 65,000 per NCS; progression includes specialist casework divisions, advocacy-focused roles, and senior management. 

Employers & settings: CPS Areas and specialist units; court-based prosecution work with police liaison. 

Key skills: Evidence evaluation, decision-making, ethics, courtroom skills, and collaboration with investigators. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Typical hours 36-38/week with rota work possible; stress from high caseloads, serious offences, and disclosure/fairness duties. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Specialist prosecutors (counter-terrorism, serious fraud), government litigation roles, or judicial application pathways. 

Official sources: NCS crown prosecutor profile; CPS careers and decision-making guidance. 

In-house Counsel / Corporate Counsel / General Counsel
Role definition & duties: Provides legal advice and risk management within an organisation (contracting, governance, regulatory issues, disputes, strategic legal input). The role commonly includes distinct ethical pressures around identifying the "client" within a complex organisation. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Generally qualified solicitor/barrister/legal executive with practising requirements maintained; regulatory responsibilities for in-house solicitors are outlined by SRA-linked guidance and Law Society practice notes. 

Salary & progression: Unspecified in primary official sources used here (significant variation by sector, organisation size, and seniority). 

Employers & settings: Corporates, charities, local authorities, government agencies; work is office-based but can include crisis response (e.g., litigation, cyber incidents). 

Key skills: Commercial awareness, stakeholder management, prioritisation, and ethical independence. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Often business-cycle driven; stress from competing stakeholder demands and urgent risk decisions. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Compliance leadership, company secretariat, data protection/privacy counsel, procurement/commercial leadership. 

Official sources: Law Society in-house ethics framework and SRA in-house guidance. 

Compliance Officer for Legal Practice / Finance & Administration (COLP/COFA)
Role definition & duties: Senior compliance roles within SRA-authorised firms responsible for regulatory compliance and reporting, ensuring systems and controls meet regulatory expectations. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Requires approval/recognition in firms regulated by the SRA; duties and guidance are specified by the SRA and professional guidance (COLP/COFA obligations). 

Salary & progression: Unspecified (depends on firm size, risk profile, and whether combined with fee-earning). 

Employers & settings: SRA-authorised law firms and ABS entities; often combined with senior management roles. 

Key skills: Regulatory knowledge, risk management, governance, audit mentality, and independence. 

Hours/conditions & stress: High accountability and regulator-facing stress; peaks around audits, incidents, and reporting obligations. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: AML officer/MLRO, data protection officer, risk director, legal operations. 

Official sources: SRA COLP/COFA responsibilities guidance; Law Society compliance overview. 

Specialist lawyer tracks (examples)
These are practice-area specialisations for qualified solicitors/barristers (and sometimes legal executives with relevant practice rights). The qualification route is therefore the underlying solicitor/barrister/advocate pathway; differentiation comes from domain knowledge, case types, and employer ecosystems. 

Human rights lawyer: Typically public law/civil liberties litigation and advisory work in NGOs, charities, government, or private practice; often linked to public-interest litigation. Employers may include organisations like Liberty and government legal teams. Salary unspecified in primary sources used here. 
Family law practitioner: Focus on divorce, child arrangements, domestic abuse, financial remedies; often close interface with mediation/MIAM processes and family courts. Salary tends to follow solicitor/barrister bands; mediation accreditation frameworks exist separately for mediators. 
Tax lawyer: Advises on tax risk, HMRC disputes, transactions, investigations; employers include professional services and in-house tax/legal teams (salary unspecified in primary sources used here). 
Environmental law roles: Planning, regulatory compliance, enforcement defence, and public law challenges; roles exist in government, local authorities and private sector; often connected to regulatory policy functions. Salary unspecified in primary sources used here. 
Intellectual property lawyer (solicitor/barrister route): Litigates/advises on IP disputes; distinct from patent/trade mark attorney professions; GOV.UK notes routes to access specialist IP professionals including IP barristers. Salary unspecified in primary sources used here. 
Cyber security legal specialist / privacy counsel: Advises on UK GDPR compliance, cyber incident response, regulatory engagement (e.g., data protection), and security governance; often works closely with DPOs/security teams. 
Courts, tribunals and judiciary

Judge (England & Wales)
Role definition & duties: Judges manage and decide cases in courts/tribunals, applying law and procedure, ensuring fairness and issuing judgments and orders. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Judicial Appointments Commission selects candidates for judicial office; eligibility and routes vary by role (often requiring significant legal experience; some tribunal roles are open to non-legal specialists). 

Salary & progression: Typical salary band GBP 101,000-GBP 295,000 per NCS; progression can include leadership judicial roles and specialist jurisdictions. 

Employers & settings: Courts and tribunals within HMCTS/Judiciary structures; courtroom-based with chambers work for writing and preparation. 

Key skills: Impartiality, decision-making, legal reasoning, courtroom control, and resilience. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Typical hours 37-40/week (role dependent); stress from high-stakes decisions, public scrutiny, and complex caseloads. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Tribunal judge/member routes, recorder roles (where applicable), coroner roles, or legal adviser roles. 

Official sources: NCS judge profile; JAC role guidance. 

Magistrate (Justice of the Peace)
Role definition & duties: Volunteer judicial officeholder hearing primarily summary criminal cases and some family/civil matters in magistrates' courts; sits in panels and makes decisions on guilt, bail, sentencing with legal adviser support. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Recruitment and commitment expectations are set out in GOV.UK guidance; magistrates are trained after appointment and must meet eligibility/character requirements. 

Salary & progression: Unpaid (expenses/allowances only); progression can include chairing a bench and specialist panels (depending on jurisdiction). 

Employers & settings: Courts; sits part-time alongside other work. 

Key skills: Fairness, judgement, listening, teamwork, and confidence in decision making. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Minimum time commitment described by GOV.UK (including sittings and training); stress includes public scrutiny and the seriousness of outcomes. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Tribunal member roles, mediation, community justice work. 

Official sources: GOV.UK "become a magistrate" guidance. 

Tribunal Judge
Role definition & duties: Determines cases in specialist tribunals (e.g., employment, immigration, tax, property, mental health), often with expert/law member panels depending on jurisdiction. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: JAC describes tribunal roles and recruitment; tribunal careers guidance notes many appointments are fee-paid with minimum sitting expectations and progression to salaried roles after experience. 

Salary & progression: Often fee-paid/day-rate based (variable); progression to salaried appointments after fee-paid experience is described in tribunal career guidance. 

Employers & settings: Tribunal system; hearings can be in-person or remote depending on listing. 

Key skills: Specialist domain knowledge, fairness, evidence evaluation, and clear decision writing. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Sitting days and preparation; stress from technical complexity and vulnerable parties. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Tribunal member (specialist/lay), judge roles in courts, ombudsman investigation. 

Official sources: JAC tribunal roles; judicial careers guidance for tribunal judges. 

Tribunal Member (lay or specialist member)
Role definition & duties: Non-legal or specialist members contribute professional expertise (medical, financial, technical, community) alongside legal members/judges in certain tribunals. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: JAC highlights that many tribunal member roles do not require a legal qualification and are recruited for specialist knowledge. 

Salary & progression: Commonly fee-paid/variable. 

Employers & settings: Tribunal hearings; preparation and panel work. 

Key skills: Domain expertise, impartial judgement, teamwork, and communication. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Sitting commitments vary; stress comes from dispute intensity and impacts on parties' lives. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Magistrate, mediation, ombudsman work, policy advisory. 

Official sources: JAC non-legal roles guidance. 

Coroner (England & Wales)
Role definition & duties: Judicial officeholder investigating certain deaths and holding inquests, providing public scrutiny in defined cases; the Chief Coroner provides process guidance. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: NCS indicates assistant coroner entry generally requires at least 5 years' experience as a solicitor/barrister or CILEx Fellow; judiciary guidance summarises the legal framework and appointment process. 

Salary & progression: Unspecified in the primary official sources used here (appointments and pay vary by coroner area). 

Employers & settings: Coroner areas (often local authority funded), courts/inquest venues, liaison with police, pathologists, families. 

Key skills: Sensitivity, evidence assessment, judicial decision-making, and public-facing communication. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Work can be emotionally demanding, involving sudden deaths and distressed families; sitting day expectations exist for assistants in guidance. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Judicial careers, medico-legal roles, public law litigation. 

Official sources: Chief Coroner/judiciary guidance and NCS coroner profile. 

Court Legal Adviser / Court Clerk (Magistrates' courts and beyond)
Role definition & duties: Advises magistrates and judges on law and procedure; NCS lists "court clerk" as an alternative title. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Typically legally qualified (or at academic stages of solicitor/barrister training for trainee routes) and trained through court legal adviser programmes; HMCTS is referenced for trainee pathways. 

Salary & progression: Typical salary band GBP 32,000-GBP 53,000; progression to senior legal adviser/justices' clerk roles or broader court leadership. 

Employers & settings: HM Courts & Tribunals Service environments; courtroom and office work. 

Key skills: Procedural rigour, rapid legal analysis, clear communication, and impartiality. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Typical hours 37-40/week; stress includes court operational deadlines and responsibility for correct legal guidance. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Judge/tribunal roles, court management, prosecution roles. 

Official sources: National Careers Service (court legal adviser). 

Court Usher
Role definition & duties: Supports hearings by preparing courtrooms, managing attendance, calling witnesses/defendants, administering oaths, handling evidence logistics, and updating records. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Entry typically via direct application and on-the-job training within court operations. 

Salary & progression: Typical salary band GBP 24,000-GBP 27,000; progression into senior court administration or listings/case progression roles. 

Employers & settings: HMCTS courts and hearing centres. 

Key skills: Calm under pressure, organisation, communication, and attention to court protocol. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Typical hours 36-38/week, occasional evenings; stress from live-hearing pressures and dealing with anxious parties. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Court administrative assistant, court security roles, victim/witness liaison. 

Official sources: National Careers Service (court usher). 

Court Administrative Assistant / Court Administrator
Role definition & duties: Day-to-day running of courts: handling enquiries, booking courtrooms, preparing daily lists, ensuring paperwork flow for judges/lawyers, issuing orders, updating systems, scanning/filing, rota coordination. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Entry via administrative routes and HMCTS recruitment; training is organisational. 

Salary & progression: Typical salary band GBP 24,000-GBP 27,000; progression into court officer, listings, case progression, or broader justice admin. 

Employers & settings: HMCTS offices and courts. 

Key skills: Accuracy, confidentiality, digital records, customer service, and coordination. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Typical hours 37-39/week; stress from high throughput and operational deadlines. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Legal adviser support roles, victim care administration, case officer roles. 

Official sources: National Careers Service (court administrative assistant). 

Legal Translator / Public Service Interpreter (courts, tribunals, police)
Role definition & duties: Interpreters convert spoken language; in courts/tribunals they support fair participation during hearings and (sometimes, with permission) related discussions. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: A major professional register is the National Register of Public Service Interpreters (NRPSI), which sets joining criteria and qualification/experience requirements by language and status. 

Salary & progression: NCS lists interpreter salary as variable; earnings depend on freelance vs employed status, language demand, and booking volume. 

Employers & settings: Freelance frameworks supplying courts/tribunals, police, healthcare, and public services; work can be in-person or remote. 

Key skills: Language mastery, neutrality, confidentiality, and ability to handle specialist terminology under pressure. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Often irregular/on-demand; stress from live, high-stakes hearings and emotionally difficult evidence. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Legal translation, conference interpreting, sign-language interpreting, specialist domains (criminal, family, immigration). 

Official sources: GOV.UK court/tribunal interpreter guidance and NRPSI joining criteria; HMCTS information on interpreting in courts. 

Bailiff / Enforcement Agent / High Court Enforcement Officer (HCEO)
Role definition & duties: Enforces court orders/debt recovery processes (serving notices, collecting debts, taking control of goods where legally permitted). NCS uses "bailiff" with alternative titles including enforcement agent and HCEO. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Certificated enforcement agents require a court-issued certificate; GOV.UK provides the EAC1 certification process and a public register. High Court enforcement is governed by regulations and authorisation by the Lord Chancellor (delegated arrangements described in consultations and regulations). 

Salary & progression: Typical NCS salary band GBP 18,000-GBP 40,000 (varies significantly by employer model and role). 

Employers & settings: County court bailiff roles (public sector), private enforcement firms, HCEO practices; work is field-based in homes/businesses. 

Key skills: Conflict de-escalation, legal process knowledge, accurate documentation, personal safety awareness. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Typical hours 35-40/week with shifts; stress and risk include confrontations and lone-working. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Debt advice roles, court operations, compliance investigation. 

Official sources: NCS bailiff profile; GOV.UK enforcement agent certification; High Court Enforcement Officers Regulations and MoJ consultation material. 

Policing, border and enforcement

Police Officer (constable)
Role definition & duties: Maintains public safety, investigates crime, supports victims, uses legal powers proportionately, and prepares cases for prosecution. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Entry routes and training pathways (including degree-holder and apprenticeship-style entry routes) are set out by the College of Policing. 

Salary & progression: Typical NCS salary band GBP 30,000-GBP 48,000; progression includes specialist units, promotion to sergeant/inspector ranks, and detective pathways. 

Employers & settings: Territorial police forces; community patrols, custody suites, stations, and multi-agency settings. 

Key skills: Communication, judgement, resilience, evidence handling, and ethical decision making. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Shift work and on-call patterns common; stress from confrontation risk and trauma exposure. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Detective, cybercrime investigator, intelligence analyst, neighbourhood policing leadership. 

Official sources: NCS police officer profile; College of Policing entry route guidance. 

Detective / Criminal Investigator (police)
Role definition & duties: Leads investigations into serious/complex crime, interviews suspects/witnesses, manages evidence, builds case files, and works with prosecutors/specialists. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Typically entered after becoming a police officer, with investigative training pathways managed within forces and aligned to policing professional frameworks. 

Salary & progression: Follows police pay structures (role allowances may apply; salary not separately specified in primary sources used here). 

Employers & settings: Police forces; CID/safeguarding/major crime units; multi-agency collaboration. 

Key skills: Investigation management, critical thinking, interviewing, disclosure discipline, and resilience. 

Hours/conditions & stress: On-call/unsocial hours; high stress due to serious offence exposure and time-critical operations. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Cybercrime investigations, financial crime, covert policing, intelligence roles. 

Official sources: College of Policing entry framework (as a base policing pathway) and CPS charging/prosecution interfaces. 

Police Community Support Officer (PCSO)
Role definition & duties: Uniformed community safety role focused on visible patrol, community engagement, reassurance, intelligence gathering, and lower-level enforcement powers depending on force policy. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Entry via force recruitment with training; can be a stepping stone to constable roles. 

Salary & progression: Typical NCS salary band GBP 24,000-GBP 28,000. 

Employers & settings: Police forces, neighbourhood teams. 

Key skills: Communication, confidence, public interaction, observation, and problem solving. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Shift work; stress from dealing with conflict in public spaces. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Police officer entry, community safety partnerships, youth engagement roles. 

Official sources: NCS PCSO profile. 

Intelligence Analyst (criminal intelligence analyst)
Role definition & duties: Uses data and intelligence to detect/prevent crime and protect security/economic interests; supports operational decision-making. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Entry via analytics/intelligence routes in policing, government agencies, or private security; may include structured analyst training. 

Salary & progression: Typical salary band GBP 20,000-GBP 52,000. 

Employers & settings: Police forces, NCA, border/security agencies, local authority intelligence hubs. 

Key skills: Analytical reasoning, data handling, communication, ethical judgement, and confidentiality. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Can require evenings/weekends at short notice; stress from time-critical threat assessments. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Cyber threat intelligence, fraud intelligence, counter-terrorism analysis. 

Official sources: NCS intelligence analyst profile. 

Border Force Officer (customs and immigration controls)
Role definition & duties: Protects UK borders at ports/airports by enforcing immigration and customs rules and handling border risks. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Recruited and trained through Home Office operational pathways; role purpose is described in official Border Force careers information. 

Salary & progression: Typical salary band GBP 28,000-GBP 34,000. 

Employers & settings: Border Force at airports, seaports, rail terminals; shift-based frontline environment. 

Key skills: Decision making, communication, vigilance, and safeguarding awareness. 

Hours/conditions & stress: Typical hours 38-42/week with shifts; stress from fast decisions and high consequence safeguarding/security issues. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Customs enforcement specialisms, investigations, intelligence, immigration enforcement. 

Official sources: NCS Border Force officer profile; Home Office Border Force role information. 

Immigration Officer
Role definition & duties: Makes decisions on whether people have the right to visit or stay in the UK, applying immigration rules and processes. 

Qualifications, training routes, bodies: Recruited through government services/civil service pathways with training in immigration decision-making. 

Salary & progression: Typical salary band GBP 30,000-GBP 36,000. 

Employers & settings: Home Office immigration functions; shift work and operational decision environments.

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Border Force, immigration enforcement investigations, asylum casework, detention operations. 

Official sources: NCS immigration officer profile; Home Office operational function information.

Hours/conditions & stress: Typical hours 37-39/week; stress from vulnerable clients, deadlines, and high-stakes outcomes. 

Adjacent roles & specialisations: Immigration solicitor route, legal aid immigration, asylum policy roles. 

Official sources: NCS immigration adviser profile; GOV.UK IAA registration resources and register.

Suggested next steps for choosing a career in law and justice
Clarify which "core work mode" suits you: adversarial advocacy, advisory problem solving, frontline operational enforcement, investigation and evidence, rehabilitation and behaviour change, or policy/system design. The day-to-day differences between, for example, solicitor casework, court legal adviser work, policing shifts, and probation risk management are substantial and should drive your initial filtering more than titles alone. 

Build a "minimum viable evidence base" before committing to long training routes: 2-4 short experiences (shadowing, volunteering, structured placements) typically provide more signal than further speculation. Examples include volunteering with victim services, observing courts/tribunals where permitted, pro bono/legal advice clinics, and participating in restorative/mediation or community safety programmes. 

Choose an education route aligned to your risk tolerance on cost and time. For solicitors, the SQE pathway explicitly requires a degree (or equivalent) and two years' QWE; for barristers, the practising certificate and pupillage pathway remains a critical bottleneck; for CILEX, "earn as you learn" routes and staged professional qualification options can be attractive where you want earlier paid legal work. 

Prioritise "employability capital" skills that transfer across sectors: structured writing, evidence handling, interviewing, stakeholder management, and disciplined confidentiality practices. These are repeatedly central across prosecution, courts, probation, intelligence and compliance functions. 

Network via the right institutions for your target pathway. For regulated professions, engage early with the core regulator/professional body materials (SRA/BSB/CILEX/IPReg/FMC/RJC) and with major public-sector employers that publish structured early talent schemes (e.g., GLD legal trainee pathways).
